Varsity Show (1937)

From my Facebook archives, November 8, 2014:

Our next movie night: Varsity Brothers -- a Warner Brothers musical from 1938 that is part of a collection of Busby Berkeley films we bought a few months ago. Dick Powell stars and sings of course. This is also one of the few films to feature performances by the great dancer John W. Bubbles, who strongly influenced Fred Astaire.

Another Astaire connection in this film is the comedian Walter Catlett. Catlett co-started with Fred and Adele in one of their earliest Broadway hits, Lady Be Good, in 1924-25.

Varsity Show was fun, light entertainment. I believe the only Busby Berkeley-staged number is the finale. It's got a college marching band theme and is much less psychedelic than some of his earlier productions, but is fun to watch. A major plus is its opening: a wonderful song-and-dance routine by Buck and Bubbles.

As for the rest of the movie, it is amusing and enjoyable, though not necessarily particularly memorable.

Having Wonderful Time (1938)

From my Facebook archives, November 7, 2014:

We're watching Having Wonderful Time tonight, a Ginger Rogers comedy from 1938. If I've counted right, this will be the 24th of her movies that we'll have seen. That only leaves nearly 50 more to go!

Note some of the names in smaller roles in this film: Ann Miller, Red Skelton, and Lucille Ball. Very early in each of their careers.

The first time I heard of this movie was early in the year while reading the bio of Hermes Pan.  Apparently he did some work on this movie, though the several musical numbers that apparently spent weeks in rehearsal are not in the final film. Later he helped George Stevens, who was directing some re-shoots, to stage some "energetically unobtrusive background dancing"  to liven up a "particularly dull dialogue scene". This was all right after he won an academy award for his work on Fred Astaire's 'Damsel in Distress'.

---

In response to a question about how many Ginger Rogers films we planned to watch, I wrote: "Planning to watch as many as we can get our hands on. The same goes for all Fred Astaire's movies. And Judy Garland too. We haven't seen any Esther Williams films yet except for her routine in Ziegfeld Follies, but we have some our list to try out."

The next day: "We finished the movie last night and I thought it was an enjoyable and funny movie, with a rather unique and interesting setting -- a vacation camp that resembles a singles club. Some of the comments by the male camp employees about the women customers left us with our mouths agape. Unfortunately such blatantly disrespectful attitudes are not as out-of-date as many would like to believe.

"I also enjoyed the location shots in the mountains outside Los Angeles. A couple decades ago, I spent quite some time tramping about those landscapes. Seeing that scenery brought back pleasant memories."

Belle of New York (1952)

From my Facebook archives, October 30, 2014:

We're slowly working our way through watching all of Fred Astaire's musical films. The latest, from 1952, The Belle of New York...


As for Belle of New York... It was not one of Fred's best films. The dancing is superb of course. The music, though, did not resonate with me. The comedy also did not work for me. And the plot makes Royal Wedding look like an epic novel. 

Fred does have a solo near the end that makes up for everything!

Gold Diggers of 1937 (1936)

Starting in late 2014, for several months, I posted occasionally on Facebook about old Hollywood films that I was watching. This was barely a year after I’d discovered Fred and Ginger, so I was still a relative newbie.

To preserve these very brief essays, and help me renew my writing habit, I will post them here, starting with my very first such post.

From my Facebook archives, October 20, 2014:

Watching Dick Powell as a life insurance salesman in 'Gold Diggers of 1937' giving a pep talk to his fellow salesmen by singing "You'll get pie in the sky when you die, die, die."  Ridiculously hilarious!


Final verdict on GD of 1937! Very enjoyable and funny film. Lots of great comic acting by the entire cast. But the songs and musical numbers overall aren't up to the standards of the earlier Busby Berkeley musicals I've seen, including both GD of 1933 and 1935, plus 42nd Street, Footlight Parade, Dames. 

In short: I liked it. But it's not an all-time classic like Gold Diggers of 1933.

Favorite actresses and actors of classic Hollywood

The single event that kick-started my interest in classic Hollywood was my discovery of Preston Sturges' great films of the 1940s very early in this decade. I was always searching for good, fun, diverting comedies to watch, and we had pretty much exhausted the limited supply of modern ones.

But I didn't really become a full-fledged classic Hollywood fan until my discovery of Fred Astaire a couple years later, in September 2013. The previously mysterious Ginger Rogers--who I of course had heard of, but alas had never seen a film of prior to late 2013--very soon became my favorite actress.

As I explored more and more films from the 1930s and 1940s, my list of favorites grew. My list has changed modestly over these last few years, but currently these are my favorite actors and actresses of that era, and hence of all time.

Actresses:

Ginger Rogers still is at the top of my list. Judy Garland, as the only other actress I know besides Ginger who was fabulous at four talents--drama, comedy, singing, dancing--has been in the next position for years, but Ida Lupino is up there with her now. Amazingly it wasn't until 2017 that I started seeing a significant number of Ida's films. I had taken notice of her right away, but full appreciation of her work took a while to break into my consciousness. Glenda Farrell, despite not getting many lead roles, is next thanks to her brilliant comic persona.

The next tier consists of Joan Blondell, Jean Arthur, Barbara Stanwyck, Bette Davis. On occasion I think Myrna Loy and Kay Francis may graduate into this tier, but I don't think I can put them there yet. Alice Brady deserves an honorable mention as well.

Actors:

Fred Astaire is obviously at the top. William Powell was an earlier discovery, though, as I found My Man Godfrey and The Thin Man a year or two before the Astaire-Rogers movies. Right below these two are Edward G. Robinson, James Cagney, Cary Grant, Dick Powell, Lionel Barrymore, and Edward Everett Horton. Honorable mention goes to Jack Carson, Joel McCrea, Don Ameche, and perhaps a few others.

Oct 13, 2017

A beginning

The purpose of this writing platform is to provide me a place to put my thoughts on subjects related to classic films and "Old Hollywood"--that semi-mythical place that existed from, roughly, the 1920s through the 1950s. Of course I may venture onto other, hopefully somewhat related, subjects at times.

As I've learned, Facebook isn't really an useful place to collect such thoughts, as it is best for personal notes and photos. Twitter has a great community of classic film fans, but doesn't provide the space to write much beyond the briefest thoughts. Far worse, those are platforms with huge problems related to abuse and fake accounts. Furthermore, they can lock you out or otherwise restrict access to your writings at any time, now or down the road.

So back to the Web.

I've blogged before, mostly about political topics, back in the heyday of personal blogging in the mid-2000s, before the rise of social media. So I realize it can be a lot of work to do well. It also can be very rewarding, and I hope to be able to get back to it periodically.
