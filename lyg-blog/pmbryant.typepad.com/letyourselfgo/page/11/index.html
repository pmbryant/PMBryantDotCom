The Fabulous Dorseys (1947)

From my Facebook archives, May 17, 2015:

How about a bio-pic where the subjects play their own characters, acting out a broadly accurate, yet fictionalized version of their careers? That's The Fabulous Dorseys, a United Artists picture from 1947.

So don't watch this for the acting quality, but do watch for some excellent music from the swing era -- which I can't get enough of lately.

Tommy and Jimmy Dorsey perform, of course, but also you can see Art Tatum, Charlie Barnet, Helen O'Connell and others.

Interestingly, Frank Sinatra spent a couple of his early years singing with Tommy Dorsey's band but he is nowhere to be seen or heard from in this film.

On The Town (1948)

From my Facebook archives, May 17, 2015:
 
On the Town. M-G-M, 1949. This musical has in its cast Gene Kelly, Frank Sinatra, Vera-Ellen, Ann Miller, and Betty Garrett. With names like these, and its status as a "classic", I was expecting a better movie.

It was modestly enjoyable. But, looking back at it, the music is forgettable, the dancing is not very interesting, the acting is weak, and the storyline is not compelling.

Most of the numbers are group pieces, so Sinatra's singing and Kelly's dancing are watered down by the weaker singers and dancers in the ensemble. And Ann Miller should have had the lead female role. She is far more charismatic than Vera-Ellen and deserved more screen time.

Some aspects of the film I particularly enjoyed were the color location shots of New York City in 1949, Ann Miller's tap dancing, seeing Betty Garrett in her younger days (after originally knowing her as a recurring character on All in the Family), the characterization of the women as the aggressive, wise ones while the men were naive, the gender-stereotype-reversed scene where taxi driver Betty Garrett is strongly hitting on a reluctant Frank Sinatra.

It seems to be a repeatable pattern that I am disappointed at the legendary Freed unit MGM musicals from the late '40s and early '50s. Many love them and sing their praises loudly, but these movies don't seem to suit my musical film tastes well. Of course Easter Parade is an exception!

The Bride Came C.O.D. (1941)

From my Facebook archives, May 16, 2015:

The Bride Came C.O.D. is a 1941 Warner Bros. film that stars James Cagney and Bette Davis. So naturally it is a screwball comedy. Ok, maybe not naturally. But that's what it is.

I read that "more recent fans" consider this film one of the low points of each of their careers. But it's actually a fun film. Not an all-time-great, but definitely enjoyable. It's fun seeing these two in unusual type of roles. Both are very good at it even though it's not their normal specialty.

Jack Carson is in it as well, which always helps.

I'll Be Seeing You (1944)

From my Facebook archives, May 11, 2015:

I'll Be Seeing You. Selznick, 1944. This wartime romantic drama, starring Ginger Rogers and Joseph Cotten, is yet another of Ginger's films that deserves to be more widely recognized as a classic. Yet it clearly has a fair number of devoted fans. I had heard virtually universally positive things about this movie since learning about it last year. And I was not disappointed when we finally watched it last week.

It certainly is a tear-jerker. But not in an manipulative way. Ginger plays a woman on a Christmas furlough from prison, while Joseph Cotten plays a traumatized soldier on a similar furlough from a psychiatric hospital. They meet and then the story unfolds.

The supporting cast, including a teenage Shirley Temple and the always-welcome Spring Byington, is excellent. With the Christmas and New Years setting, this has deservedly become a favorite holiday film of many of those who have been fortunate enough to discover it.

Dance, Girl, Dance (1940): Lucille Ball pre-television

From my Facebook archives, May 7, 2015:

Dance, Girl, Dance. This 1940 RKO film was directed by Dorothy Arzner, making it one of very few Hollywood movies directed by women back in those days. (Unfortunately there aren't exactly a huge number these days.)

Maureen O'Hara stars but Lucille Ball really makes the movie. It's an enjoyable film, but without Lucy's performance would be a bit blah. So watch it to see Lucy in a great, early career role, as a scheming, ambitious burlesque dancer.

Lucy's pre-television career is oddly fascinating, given the mega-star she became later. I hadn't known anything about it until we learned (after watching the movie) that she had a non-speaking role in the fashion show at the end of Roberta. And that she was in a few other Fred and Ginger movies as well, though hard to recognize unless you're paying close attention. In Follow The Fleet she's almost recognizable, has a few lines, and appears in several scenes. So I found her right away there. In Roberta we found her on the 2nd viewing. But in Top Hat, even after knowing she was in it and had one line, I missed her on the 2nd viewing. We recently watched that for 3rd time and I finally spotted her.

The Lost Weekend (1945)

From my Facebook archives, May 2, 2015:

The Lost Weekend. Paramount. 1945. This Billy Wilder-directed film was certainly a change from our typical fare. It is a dark film that chronicles a few days in the life of a near-hopeless alcoholic.

Films with similar themes have been fairly common in recent decades, but this was apparently one of the first. Ray Milland was excellent, as he had to be--he *is* the film.

The score is on the over-dramatic side, and includes Twilight Zone-like sound effects from a theremin. Such sound effects later became strongly associated with science fiction stories so they seem odd in this very earthbound movie. An article I found suggested the score was a reaction to an initial screening, with a more jazzy soundtrack, where the audience laughed at many scenes. With the revised score, it is impossible to think there is any comedy hidden in this film.

The Lost Weekend is a very good film overall. Though it is a reminder of why I prefer lighter fare. Even with an optimistic, if uncertain, Hollywood ending, you don't come away from this film feeling uplifted.

Together Again (1944)

From my Facebook archives, April 30, 2015:
 
Together Again. Columbia, 1944. The title of this romantic comedy film refers to Irene Dunne and Charles Boyer being reunited for their third film. I imagine the first two must have been pretty good, though I haven't yet seen them. This one wouldn't merit a re-match by itself.

The film is funny at times, but it has a pervasive attitude that Irene Dunne's character is severely misguided by serving as a small-town mayor and living on her own, rather than finding a man--any man--marrying him and quitting her job. The film beats you over the head with it. As a result, Charles Coburn, whose character is particularly forceful about those attitudes, isn't very funny for once.

The soundtrack is also annoying, with musical effects that attempt to emphasize the comedy, but just distract from it. I've noticed similar soundtracks in other films of the mid-to-late '40s. It seems to have been an unfortunate trend. It's also too reminiscent of mediocre television sitcoms from the 1960s.

Despite all that I still liked this film a little better than Irene Dunne's earlier film Theodora Goes Wild, with which it is paired on a DVD and which I wrote about a few weeks ago. The young characters in this film, played by Mona Freeman and Jerome Courtland, are quite amusing.

Only Angels Have Wings (1939): Not enough Jean Arthur

From my Facebook archives, April 28, 2015:

I was reading a very good biography of Jean Arthur earlier this month, called "The Actress Nobody Knew". So it seemed an opportune time to see another Jean Arthur film. (Then again, isn't any time?) The one we had handy was Only Angels Have Wings, Columbia, 1939.

Cary Grant is her co-star, and Rita Hayworth makes a brief, early career appearance.

The major problem with this film is that there isn't nearly enough Jean Arthur in it. Her role is surprisingly small. This is a male-bonding film. It feels like a war film, though technically not set during wartime.

It was a modestly enjoyable film for what it was, though it was hard to swallow the premise of pilots taking extreme risks just to deliver mail.

And, alas, not enough Jean Arthur.

Private Buckaroo (1942): Helen Forrest and the Andrews Sisters

From my Facebook archives, April 25, 2015:

Discovering Fred Astaire a year-and-half ago has led me to discover several other amazing performers. Ginger Rogers, of course! But also Artie Shaw and his band. And through them, the wonderful singer Helen Forrest.

Trying to learn more about her, I found a clip of a captivating song, "You Made Me Love You", performed by Harry James and his band and sung by Helen. I could go on-and-on about that musical scene. But for now, it's relevant in that it is the opening for the 1942 Universal film "Private Buckaroo" and so inspired us to watch that film last week. It turned out we already owned a copy in an inexpensive set of 50 public domain musicals that we had bought last Christmas.

As wartime propaganda musical comedy extravaganzas, Private Buckaroo beats Stage Door Canteen, which I wrote about recently, hands down. Not only does it have Harry James' excellent big band and Helen Forrest (though not nearly enough of her), but it also features several performances by the Andrews Sisters. Of course, I'd heard some of their songs before and knew they were extremely popular in the '40s. But I'd never actually seen them perform. And it turns out they are very fun to watch, particularly Patty.

So watch this film for the musical performances, which are fortunately plentiful. With the comedy of Shemp Howard and Mary Wickes and Joe E. Lewis thrown in, the silly propaganda plot barely gets in the way at all.
