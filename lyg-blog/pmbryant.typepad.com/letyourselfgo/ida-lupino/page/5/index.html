Article on Ida Lupino's "Outrage"

Here is an interesting article about Outrage, one of the most notable of the films directed by Ida Lupino.

By Anne Morra of The Museum of Modern Art.

Excerpt:

“As a producer and director, Lupino ran her own show; she had a director’s chair on set with “Mother” emblazoned on the back. This independence allowed her to take risks on narrative films that depicted real people in real situations, with messy lives, broken hearts, and darkened psyches. But with Lupino at the helm, these postwar American lives seemed unquestionably authentic.”

Article link: Ida Lupino's "Outrage", by Anne Morra

Ida Lupino on the demise of her independent production company, The Filmakers

Ida Lupino, in a 1967 essay:

Filmakers was doing fine but we made one fatal mistake. We were talked into the distribution business. I opposed them [her business partners, Collier Young and Malvin Wald] every step of the way. We were creative people; we were picture-making people, I argued. ‘We know nothing about distribution. Let’s stay away from it.’ I was outvoted, and pretty soon we were out of business.

The films directed by Ida Lupino, 1949-1966

Films directed by Ida Lupino:

  • Not Wanted (1949), uncredited
  • Never Fear (1949), aka The Young Lovers
  • Outrage (1950)
  • Hard, Fast and Beautiful (1951)
  • The Hitch-Hiker (1953)
  • The Bigamist (1953)
  • The Trouble with Angels (1966)

All but the last were with her production company, The Filmakers---originally known as Emerald Productions.

In most of these she was additionally involved as writer, producer, or actor.

Ida Lupino's relationship with Humphrey Bogart in "High Sierra" (1941)

After her huge success in They Drive By Night, Ida Lupino was cast in High Sierra and moved up to top billing before the film was released.

Initially there was some friction between Ida and Humphrey Bogart on the set.

“I have a way of kidding with a straight face; so has Bogie. Neither of us recognized the trait in the other. Each of us thought the other was being nasty, and we were both offended.”

As they worked together, though, she grew to appreciate Bogart, as he helped her through some tough scenes. She later recalled, “He was the most loyal, wonderful guy in the world.”

Ida Lupino in "They Drive By Night" (1940)

Ida Lupino in They Drive By Night (1940). Also starring George Raft, Humphrey Bogart, and Ann Sheridan, with Alan Hale in a supporting role.

Warner Bros. producer Mark Hellinger—-impressed by Ida’s performance in Paramount’s The Light That Failed opposite Ronald Colman—-wanted Ida for this role. Director Raoul Walsh was ready to sign up another actress, when Ida gave a screen test arranged by Hellinger that sold studio boss Jack Warner on her.

This role and film launched Ida’s great career at Warner Bros. that lasted through much of the 1940s, before she finally decided to make her own films.

Ida Lupino on acting after directing

I did not abandon my acting [after starting on a production and directing career]. Whenever producers offered me screen roles, I accepted, for even if I enjoyed being a lone woman in the world of movie executives, it was fun to be a pampered, fussed-over actress. I loved playing sexy, warm dames who are tough in life, who do not let life affect them, very much myself!”

— Ida Lupino, in the foreword to Ida Lupino: Beyond the Camera by Mary Ann Anderson with Ida Lupino.

Ida Lupino on women's presence in classic Hollywood

Women, according to these stalwart defenders of male superiority [in the 1940s and 1950s], may not be film musical directors, cameramen, set designers, composers, assistant directors, directors, or production managers. The only sort of job encouragement some women got in the early days of Hollywood was the portrayal of women on the screen. Sometimes, I suspect we were resented for even this intrusion.

— Ida Lupino, in the foreword to Ida Lupino: Beyond the Camera by Mary Ann Anderson with Ida Lupino.

Ida Lupino's years at Paramount: 1933-1937

Ida Lupino moved to Hollywood from London in 1933, at the age of fifteen. She was already a film veteran, and then-blonde Ida was signed by Paramount as a candidate for the lead in Alice in Wonderland. That did not pan out.

“You cannot play naive if you’re not. I never had any childhood,” Ida said.

But she was featured in numerous films at Paramount over the next few years, starting with Search for Beauty with Buster Crabbe. In 1937, at 19, in search of more substantive roles, she left her contract and went free-lance.

This did not work out immediately and she was off-screen for over a year. But of course in the long run, her move payed off tremendously and she became a top star at Warner Bros. in the 1940s.

Ida Lupino on the "poor man's Bette Davis" quote

I never said I was the poor man's Bette Davis. Warner Bros. came up with that bad line, not me. I respected Davis. I did say jokingly that Bette Davis could do the housework!

– Ida Lupino, in Ida Lupino: Beyond the Camera by Mary Ann Anderson with Ida Lupino
