Pin Up Girl (1944)

From my Facebook archives, February 10, 2015:

Pin Up Girl. 20th Century Fox, 1944. This colorful Betty Grable musical was quite a change of pace from our last film, Public Enemy. Though Betty Grable's character is rather deceitful, really.

Anyway the movie doesn't take its own story seriously. It doesn't have any plot resolution worth speaking of, and since we don't grow to care for the characters, that's fine. The film is just an excuse for big musical production numbers, several of them fun.

I particularly liked the big band swing music of Charlie Spivak and his orchestra. Hermes Pan, Fred Astaire's long-time collaborator and the dance director for this film, made an on-screen appearance to dance with Betty. That must have been why this movie was in my queue, though I had forgotten that.

Another interesting item is getting to see performances from Martha Raye as a young woman.

The final military march number was uninteresting, over-long, and out-of-place. But this was during World War 2.

The Public Enemy (1931)

From my Facebook archives: February 7, 2015:

The Public Enemy. Warner Brothers, 1931.

This classic gangster film is rather different than our typical movie watching experience, but, as good as they were, I can't have the only Cagney films I've seen be the musicals Footlight Parade and Yankee Doodle Dandy.

Now I've seen Cagney in the sinister type of role that defined his career. It was an excellent film, but far darker than I normally like.

As a bonus Joan Blondell has a small role. Jean Harlow has a significant part, but I didn't find her compelling.

Mae Clarke, who is amazingly uncredited, was far more interesting. And she also had to put up with Cagney shoving a grapefruit into her face!

Funny Face (1957)

From my Facebook archives, January 31, 2015:

Funny Face, Paramount 1957. Fred Astaire. Audrey Hepburn. Well, Audrey Hepburn. Fred Astaire. That's the starring order.

Audrey asked for Fred to be her co-star in this, her first musical--and she got him. There were apparently some contractual difficulties putting it all together, but, as Fred put it in his autobiography: "I was not missing it. Period."

The film certainly has a compelling visual style to it. It immediately looks different than the previous Fred films we've been watching--the 60s appear to have arrived even though it's 1957.

Overall the film was enjoyable--more so than The Band Wagon for me--though it is well down the list of my favorite Fred films. Several of the dance numbers are fun, particularly Fred's solo and the comic duet with Kay Thompson. But the romantic duets between the two stars are relatively weak by Fred's standards.

Kay Thompson, who amongst other things was a vocal coach for Judy Garland, was great to watch. I have no clue why she stayed mostly behind the scenes and didn't perform in more movies.

Broadway Bad (1933)

From my Facebook archives, January 25, 2015:

Broadway Bad, a Fox film from early 1933, is the latest Ginger Rogers film we've watched: number 28. Along with the near-simultaneous 42nd Street, this is the earliest in her career we've seen. Ginger's role is rather small; fortunately the star, Joan Blondell, is always enjoyable.

This is definitely pre-code, with single motherhood playing such a large role in the plot.

Here's a blog that shows what movies were playing in Milwaukee the week of March 12, 1933. Ginger was in three of them: 42nd Street (held over for being such a big hit), Broadway Bad, and The Thirteenth Guest (as the star).
http://mondo70.blogspot.com/2013/03/now-playing-march-12-1933.html?m=1

Ginger was very busy in 1933! It sure paid off. One of her last films that year was Flying Down to Rio, which launched her and Fred Astaire into superstardom.

In her book, Ginger calls Broadway Bad and one other of her many 1933 films (A Shriek in the Night) "bread-and-butter" pictures.

"They may have been B pictures, and not the most outstanding of the films I made, but they allowed me to pay the rent and put food on our table."

The Pirate (1948)

From my Facebook archives, January 12, 2015:

This film didn't do very well on its initial release, but apparently has acquired a bit of a cult following. With Judy, of course this was quite entertaining. The plot is fun as well, and the Nicholas Brothers have a routine with Gene Kelly. But still I'd have to place this well down my list of Judy's films. Tom, Dick, and Harry (1941)

From my Facebook archives, January 8, 2015:

[The photo below is] Ginger Rogers and Garson Kanin, the star and director of 'Tom, Dick, and Harry' from 1941 -- a very fun romantic comedy. George Murphy, Burgess Meredith, and Alan Marshal play the title characters. I loved the movie and Ginger's performance.

After posting that photo of Ginger and Garson Kanin, I realized that I've read a book by each of them in the last few months: Ginger's autobiography and Garson's "Hollywood", a collection of amusing essays.

As successful a director as he was, Garson Kanin appears to have been even more successful as a writer. He co-wrote, with his wife Ruth Gordon, several notable screenplays, including the Hepburn-Tracy films 'Adam's Rib' and 'Pat and Mike'. He also wrote the story on which 'The More The Merrier' was based. I posted about that excellent Jean Arthur comedy just a couple weeks back.

Lost Horizon (1937)

From my Facebook archives, January 4, 2015:

[O]ur latest movie was Lost Horizon, from 1937. Directed by Frank Capra and starring Ronald Colman. Superb movie -- loved it. And fascinated by the magnificent Art Deco palace in the middle of the Himalayas!

The version available these days is an attempt to restore some 20-plus minutes that had been cut over the years. The sound track is complete but a few minutes of the video are still missing and several more minutes are poor quality. Also the original negative had completely deteriorated by the 1960s so none of the film is at the level of quality it deserves. And this is after rather herculean (and fascinating) efforts at restoration.

The Band Wagon (1953)

From my Facebook archives: January 3, 2015:

The Band Wagon. MGM 1953. On New Years Day we finished up this film. It was our 27th Fred Astaire film, leaving only three of his musicals that we have not yet seen.

I have mixed feelings about this one. I really want to like it a lot, and it was mostly fun. But it just isn't as good as most of Fred's other films, though Fred himself is in fine form.

The best parts: the Dancing in the Dark romantic duet with Cyd Charisse, Fred's 'shoeshine' dance with LeRoy Daniels, Jack Buchanan's outrageous storytelling while pitching his character's show. The worst parts: Oscar Levant (mostly horrible here, and I normally like him!) and the staging for the 'That's Entertainment' song (embarrassingly boring for a 'dance' number that includes Fred). Overall, too much MGM, not enough Fred.

Million Dollar Mermaid (1952)

From my Facebook archives, December 31, 2014:
 
Million Dollar Mermaid, MGM 1952. Esther Williams. Victor Mature. Walter Pidgeon.

Out of curiosity, we've been meaning to see an Esther Williams musical ever since seeing her in a brief underwater routine in Ziegfeld Follies earlier this year. This is apparently one of her best so I chose this one not knowing anything else about it. It turns out Busby Berkeley, of all people, staged the swimming spectaculars. And he seems to have reverted to his early days of gigantic psychedelic numbers. Of course they were the highlight of the film.

The rest of the movie is allegedly a bio-pic about Annette Kellerman, who I had never heard of but who is a fascinating character -- an Australian who popularized swimming for women early in the 1900s (amongst other things). Though to find out her real story you'll need to read more about her elsewhere as the film is only very loosely based on her life.
