August 19, 2004

NPR too? PBS soon?

The last couple days, I noticed that NPR has been giving Kerry short shrift in their morning news segments at the top of the hour. Bush was given a substantial sound-bite, in his own voice, while Kerry was either ignored completely or paraphrased briefly by the commentator. At this late stage of the campaign, this struck me as irresponsible. Letting it happen (at least) two days in a row? Unconscionable.

But apparently I have missed the really bad stuff.

Larre at The Left Coaster has a very disturbing post about NPR's recent coverage of the Swift Boat Smear Campaign; in particular, about the work of Juan Williams and Mara Liasson. Via my own listening, I remember from back when Williams hosted the "Talk of the Nation" show that he has long played the part of the classic FOX News "Democrat"; i.e., very quick to rationalize GOP actions, while even quick to criticism Dems, so I can't claim to be shocked.

But it is depressing nonetheless.

Over the last two weeks Williams has spearheaded NPR's twice-daily repetition of the “swift boat” slanders of John Kerry’s war record. In this, he is abetted by other NPR stalwarts, most notably Mara Liasson, also of Fox News-NPR. With almost every one of her radio reports, Liasson ignores Kerry’s first Vietnam tour on the guided missile frigate U.S.S. Gridley. She invariably "reports," wrongly, that Kerry served only “four months in Vietnam” before moving on to trash his war record.

And later:

Make no mistake. The right-wing corporate types who formerly broadcast U.S. propaganda to Europe have taken over National Public Radio. Earlier this year investigative reporter Daniel Zwerdling was put out to pasture along with popular Morning Edition host Bob Edwards . The television side at the Corporation for Public Broadcasting is under the same kind of attack.

I'm afraid he may be very correct on this. As far as PBS goes, Bill Moyers' fabulous show "NOW" is being cut back to half an hour later this year, while PBS has already given shows to conservative CNN partisan hack Tucker Carlson, and apparently will soon give one to, get this, the editorial page of the Wall Street Journal. What, can these fellows not get their opinions out unless PBS gives them yet another platform?

If you aren't familiar with the writings of the editorial page of the Wall Street Journal, consider yourself very fortunate. As mainstream media goes, they are amongst the looniest of the loony conservatives.

"They tell me there are 15,000 or more people here," Mr. Kerry said, glancing up at the crowd. "I'm happy to hear that because I took the over on 11,000."

Wait, there's more. "We have to get this economy moving," Mr. Kerry said a bit later, "because there is nothing worse than an unemployed flying Elvis."

Asked aboard his campaign plane Thursday night where he gets this material, Mr. Kerry hurried back to his cabin without answering.

Equating the Kerry and Bush service records

Here's an AP article at the New York Times website that plays things pretty straight until the last few paragraphs. Then, the article concludes in a stunning display of equating Kerry's Vietnam combat service with Bush's Texas service.

The Vietnam-era military records of both candidates remain an issue in the race, with former sailors accusing Kerry of exaggerating his war record and Democrats questioning whether Bush showed up for duty in the Texas Air National Guard.

Republican Sen. John McCain of Arizona, a decorated Vietnam veteran who campaigns with Bush but has worked closely with Kerry in the Senate, has called for a cease fire.

"I wish we would stop opening wounds from a war of more than 30 years ago and talk about the war we're fighting now," McCain told The Associated Press on Tuesday. "I believe they both served honorably."

No doubt this is exactly the kind of coverage Karl Rove and Company were looking for when they tacitly encouraged the Swift Boat Smear Campaign by failing to repudiate it. This definitely calls for some letters to the editor to set the record straight.

August 17, 2004

Krugman comes through again

Of course, there are some bright spots in the media landscape, and Paul Krugman of the New York Times has been one of the brightest for several years now. Read his latest: Saving the Vote.

Everyone knows it, but not many politicians or mainstream journalists are willing to talk about it, for fear of sounding conspiracy-minded: there is a substantial chance that the result of the 2004 presidential election will be suspect.

When I say that the result will be suspect, I don't mean that the election will, in fact, have been stolen. (We may never know.) I mean that there will be sufficient uncertainty about the honesty of the vote count that much of the world and many Americans will have serious doubts.

How might the election result be suspect? Well, to take only one of several possibilities, suppose that Florida - where recent polls give John Kerry the lead - once again swings the election to George Bush.
