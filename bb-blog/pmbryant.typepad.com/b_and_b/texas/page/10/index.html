May 19, 2005

Backers of Taxpayer Extortion Act feeling the heat

A few weeks ago, Texas Agricultural Commisioner Susan Combs helped kick off the deceptive public campaign for the Texas Taxpayer Extortion Act, HB 2833, with an op-ed in the San Antonio Express-News.  Now, with the legislative session winding down rapidly, but the bill stuck in committee in the Texas state senate, the bill's sponsor, Rep. Robby Cook of Eagle Lake, apparently found the need for yet another op-ed in the same newspaper. 

The theme of the new op-ed:  this bill isn't as bad as everyone is saying.  Look at the numerous exceptions we've added!

Of course, he still peppers his essay with the rhetoric of the larger "takings" movement, so his paean to the exceptions added since Combs'  op-ed, about a pure piece of takings legislation, rings very hollow.  The larger agenda is crystal clear, and it still equates to forcing taxpayers to pay polluters not to pollute.

And as the Austin Chronicle notes today:

At a hearing before the Senate Natural Resources Committee, Jim Marston of the Environmental Defense Fund argued that HB 2833 cast too wide a net. He quoted a former Senator who said a bill with more than five exceptions was a bill that needed to be written more narrowly. So far, HB 2833 has more than a dozen exceptions for different types of regulation cities should be able to offer without penalty, with more exceptions being entertained by Staples to accommodate the concerns of even more cities.

Robby Cook's op-ed has the clear feel of desperation, and that gives me a good feeling that the public good may actually prevail in this case.  Or will Staples, Cook, Combs, and the Texas Landowners Conservancy gang manage to purchase enough votes with even more alleged exceptions?

May 18, 2005

Taxpayer Extortion Act treading water in the Texas senate

The Texas senate Natural Resources committee held a public hearing on Monday about the Taxpayer Extortion Act, which, as I mentioned earlier, passed the house last week (as HB 2833).  Apparently it is still pending in the committee, but will be up for action again "later this week." 

An update from the Austin American-Stateman today:

The legislation's supporters call it a fairly focused response to Austin's 13-year-old Save Our Springs water quality ordinance, as well as potential copycats that might come out of planning efforts in the Hill Country. Through the 1990s, legislators routinely passed such so-called Austin-bashing laws, each designed to weaken the city's strict environmental rules.

But this year, cities such as Round Rock, Houston and Plano, not usually seen as hotbeds of environmental activism, have campaigned against the measure, openly worrying that it would have consequences beyond Austin's city limits.

Concerns range from the specific — such as the possibility of zoning ordinances and other land-use regulations becoming impossibly expensive to enforce — to general worries about cities' ability to manage their growth. Lawmakers have tried to at least partly exempt San Antonio's development regulations from the measure.

The measure's Central Texas sponsors say there is no basis for the fears. Proponents have until next Wednesday, the Senate's deadline for passing most measures, to assuage their far-flung opponents.

Dan Wheelus, a lawyer and lobbyist [for the Texas Landowners Conservancy] who helped draft the measure, dismissed objections over zoning and flood control as "red-herring stuff." He told the Senate Natural Resources committee Monday, "There is one ordinance in this entire state that offends these parameters (in the proposal), and it is the Austin SOS ordinance."

But the legislation's opponents say that, intentionally or not, the measure is honeycombed with consequences for cities far beyond Austin.

"It is a threat to every taxpayer in every city," said Sen. Eliot Shapleigh, an El Paso Democrat.

The Texas Municipal League is ramping up pressure to kill the measure, and the Texas Conference of Urban Counties has expressed reservations about it. Both groups and several cities joined environmentalists in testifying against it at a Senate Natural Resources committee hearing Monday.

The committee was expected to pass the matter up to the full Senate, but members instead left it pending. It is expected to return to the committee's agenda, with a number of amendments designed to take care of some protests, later this week.

Cities and taxpayers around the state have good reason to fear this legislation.  The authors and backers of this bill have wrapped themselves and the bill itself in the language of the decades-old "takings" movement, which aims to compel the general public to pay for all sorts of measures passed for the public good that allegedy cause loss of some amount of land value to a few powerful landowners.  There is absolutely no reason to believe that the effect of this legislation would be restricted to one measure in one city.

And even if it were, it would still be an atrocious special-interest giveaway.  Remember, it is a handful of Austin-area real estate developers and land speculators who founded the Texas Landowners Conservancy a few months ago, apparently for the sole purpose of pushing this legislation.  These are the exact people who, if this bill passes, would reap huge benefits at the expense of the taxpayers in their area.

Matt at Just Another Blog says that this is "a bad bill for everyone."  But that's not quite right.  It's very very good for a very very few.

May 12, 2005

Who will stop the Taxpayer Extortion Act?

I've written a lot lately about the so-called "takings" legislation that is moving through the Texas legislature, the Taxpayer Extortion Act, and, alas, am getting quite sick of it.  Unfortunately, it is still alive, with HB 2833 having officially passed the House yesterday, so I feel compelled to continue.

The Taxpayer Extortion Act is in the hands of the Texas state senate now, a bit amended from its original version.  But remaining intact is its fundamental nature of compelling taxpayers to pay off irresponsible real estate developers to prevent those same developers from taking actions that would end up polluting aquifers that supply water to millions of people.

So I'll continue to highlight every article I find about this in the press.  Now, it's the weekly Austin Chronicle's turn:

The city of Austin and several other Central Texas cities view the bill as what it in fact is – a "taking" of local control and a "giving" of taxpayer subsidies to landowners/developers.

Cities and environmental groups from across the state have spent the past weeks, days, and now, the final hours, prevailing on senators to do the right thing. On the other hand, so have "property rights" activists. If cautiously cocky developer lobbyists are the best indicators of what to expect in the Senate, then we are apparently screwed. On a hopeful note, opponents late Tuesday were said to be close to securing the magic number of 11 senators needed to block the legislation.

...

It's a wonder that the bill even made it out of committee in the first place, given the Legislative Budget Board's gloomy analysis: "The financial impact on cities is anticipated to be substantial, especially in fast-growing areas of the state," the report warns. "Cities would have to prepare a 'takings' impact assessment for each regulation. ... Uncertainty created by the bill could result in high legal fees to determine whether appraisals are correct."

But even bad bills – rather, especially the bad bills – have a way of winning over legislators, no matter how dire or costly the projected outcome. When the bill returned to the floor for a third time on Monday, Reps. Rodriguez, Burnam, and Elliott Naishtat each made several attempts to pile on amendments that would gut, weaken, or remedy the measure, to no avail. In the end, the House, including area Reps. Terry Keel, Todd Baxter, and Mike Krusee, voted 103-41 to send Austin and its million-dollar lobby team packing.

The article goes on to discuss more information about the shady group of Austin-area real estate developers that readers of this site already are quite familiar with, the Texas Landowners Conservancy.

Certainly at least 11 state senators will keep the public's interest in mind, and kill this bill.  Certainly?

UPDATE:  Matt has more about this bill at Just Another Blog, including this:

Puente offered an amendment that was accepted that supposedly would protect San Antonio, but it does not. The Puente amendment does not protect San Antonio's water quality ordinance: it prevents San Antonio from limiting impervious cover more than 30% in the recharge zone for single familty and duplex developments, and it prevents San Antonio from limiting impervious cover more than 45% in all other circumstances. The Puente amendment does not solve any other zoning and land use problems that this bill will create for San Antonio, including restrictions associated with the River Walk and downtown preservation.

May 09, 2005

Texas Landowners Conservancy unveiled in the Austin newspaper

The Austin American-Statesman has a story on the deceptive group calling themselves the Texas Landowners Conservancy.  Of course, this is old news to regular readers of this site, as we wrote a similar account over a month ago, on April 8.  Among the revelations in this new story is that lawyers for this group actually wrote the Taxpayer Extortion Act, HB 2833 and SB 1647. 

The American-Statesman account begins:

Start with the name: the Texas Landowners Conservancy.

It evokes environmental groups, such as the Nature Conservancy and the Hill Country Conservancy, conjuring backgrounds as green and landscapes as lush as those on the LandownersConservancy's Web site.

Yet the group has created more ire and trepidation among Central Texas environmentalists, and some government officials, than any developer in a decade. In just three months of existence, the Landowners Conservancy has shown that is has deep pockets, powerful political connections and strong ties to developers, some of whom stand to reap bigger profits if the group can convince lawmakers that cities should compensate landowners for strict environmental regulations.

The group's founders develop land or own real estate companies. Yet they reject comparisons to better-known developers who repeatedly persuaded lawmakers to undermine Austin environmental regulations, particularly through the mid-1990s. Landowners Conservancy members say they take good care of their land, but they need to protect their property values from environmentalists.

Unfortunately, this article gives way too much attention to the claims of real estate developer Ted Stewart and company to be "environmentalists," and focuses very little on the deception of their rhetoric.  In fact, it completely fails to note that the web site of this organization and its various press releases have not disclosed its founders' business interests in land development.

A couple head-spinning quotes from the article:

Ted Stewart, co-founder:  "We're environmentalists. Our issue is not about (damaging the environment) at all."

That's right.  It's all about money.  Polluting water supplies is just a side effect.

Charlotte Warren, treasurer:  "We're pretty much novices at this deal.  None of us really knew what it would take to start from zero and try to have an impact."

Try to reconcile that statement with this fact from the American-Statesman article:

One [of the group's lobbyists], David Sibley, once was chairman of the Senate Business and Commerce Committee. Another, Nora Del Bosque, is the sister of a top aide to House Speaker Tom Craddick.

Novices indeed.

Stop the Texas Taxpayer Extortion Act, HB 2833. Today.

What I am calling the Taxpayer Extortion Act, HB 2833, is back on the calendar for the Texas House today.  This is the "takings" bill that would force taxpayers of certain cities to fork over huge amounts of money to irresponsible real estate developers in order to get the developers to behave responsibly and not develop properties in ways that would be harmful to water quality and other environmental factors.

Over the weekend, the San Antonio Express-News published an op-ed by the mayor of Castroville, a small town just west of San Antonio, further pointing out how harmful this legislation would be.

Everyone claims to be for local control of cities and schools. But in the Texas Legislature, local control is under assault. And as the mayor of one small town where local control has allowed us to preserve our unique heritage, I'm worried.

Just 20 minutes west of San Antonio, Castroville was founded by French Alsatian colonists in the mid-1800s, when Texas was still a republic. Today, with 97 historic European-style homes, inns and churches, Castroville is both a national and state historic district, and we enjoy a thriving tourist trade. And it's a great place to work and raise a family.

Unfortunately, a bill in the Legislature could severely impact the things that make our town unique.

If House Bill 2833, by Rep. Robby Cook, D-Eagle Lake, or its near-twin Senate Bill 1647, by Sen. Todd Staples, R-Palestine, passes and is signed into law, our town will be faced with large new administrative and legal costs and could be forced to make enormous cash payoffs to landowners who simply disagree with the land use rules we believe are necessary to preserve the unique character of Castroville.

Most Texas cities and towns have land use restrictions to preserve their safety and character. They protect historical districts and structures, and they protect property values in residential neighborhoods by specifying minimum lot sizes or requiring setbacks from the street and neighbors' homes. They limit impervious cover to ensure greenery or keep moderate rainstorms from turning into raging floods.

In our town, as in many others, we restrict where less desirable businesses like landfills, junkyards and liquor stores locate.

If HB 2833 or SB 1647 becomes law, local restrictions against such ventures could entitle a landowner to sizable compensation from our city's limited treasury. It could subject us to lawsuits by any property owner, even out-of-town speculators who don't like local rules.

Since we have a budget of just $7.1 million and only 41 town employees, we may simply have to repeal the regulations that have protected the unique character of our community.

Dozens of unique communities across the state would be similarly vulnerable: Anyone with a piece of property and an outrageously out-of-place business plan could demand compensation when a city says no. And he wouldn't have to prove any actual losses — only that the restriction prevents him from making more.

...

Local land use planning is not "taking" a person's property or freedom. These zoning and land use restrictions almost always end up raising the value of property in affected areas. These bills would make taxpayers pay compensation for setting rules that improve communities and increase property values.

Unless the citizens of Texas stop these bills, the unique character of towns like Castroville could be lost forever.

The time to stop these bills is right now.  Today.  If you live in Texas, you know what to do.

Austin state rep delays 'Taxpayer Extortion Act'

At least one elected official in the state capitol is looking out for the public interest.  Three cheers for state Rep. Eddie Rodriguez of Austin:

House Bill 2833 puts property rights ahead of local environmental rules, just as the House tends to do. It specifically targets Austin's ordinances, an old bone that lawmakers have shown no reluctance to pick. And it now carries an amendment that exempts San Antonio, the only big city outside Austin that is likely to care about it.

Yet two parliamentary moves in the past two weeks, both by an Austin state representative, have left the bill in a race against the clock.

On Monday, Democratic Rep. Eddie Rodriguez once again pulled the rug out from under HB 2833, noting a technical error in the way an analysis described the bill, which would force Austin and other cities to compensate property owners for environmental regulations that devalue their land beyond a certain point.

The so-called point of order would not have affected the bill had no one raised it. But because Rodriguez did, the bill now must be approved by the House Land and Resource Management Committee for the third time and be set on another House calendar.

Last month, Rodriguez also had the bill sent back to committee — and delayed a vote — by pointing out a technical error.

The bill could be back on the House floor as early as this week, according to its author, Rep. Robby Cook, D-Eagle Lake.

The problem is that next week, a series of legislative deadlines will start to kick in, and bills that have not been approved will start to die on the vine. That shrinking window now represents the best chance to kill the bill, at least in the House.

"I think this bill is really bad for Austin," Rodriguez said. "I have to use every weapon in my arsenal."

Alas, this 'Taxpayer Extortion Act' being pushed by, among others, GOP Agricultural Commissioner Susan Combs and the Texas Landowners Conservancy, is not dead yet.  Yet perhaps there is hope.

Texas legislature to act soon on the 'Taxpayer Extortion Act'

The Texas legislature is acting soon on the alleged "property rights" bill that in actuality forces taxpayers to pay real estate developers to behave responsibly and not pollute our water.  This is the "takings" bill being pushed by GOP Agriculture Commissioner Susan Combs and the deceptive group of real estate developers and land speculators that calls themselves the Texas Landowners Conservancy and that hides their personal monetary interests behind a false front of concern for the environment.

In fact, it appears the House may be acting today on their version, HB 2833.  Please call your state representative as soon as possible.  Information on state representatives is available at this link.

The state Senate looks to be holding a public hearing today on their version, SB 1647.  Please call your state senator as well.  Again, see this link for information on state senators.

The Greater Edwards Aquifer Alliance summarizes HB 2833 like this:

This bill would require the Cities to PAY landowners when they develop their property at less than 45% impervious cover (impervious cover is rooftops, building, roads). San Antonio, Austin, San Marcos and other cities limit impervious cover to 15% to 20% in the most sensitive areas of the watershed. This bill would make taxpayers pay the difference in profits when the city ordinances apply. The legislators behind this bill have said that taxpayers should pay developers when water quality protections cut into developer profits, and we don't have a right to clean water.

The only thing these bills do is preserve the ability of real estate developers and land speculators to make a fortune at the expense of the taxpayers' ability to protect their water quality.  Please read my three previous posts on this issue for more on the story behind this piece of legislative extortion:  Paying polluters not to pollute, What is the Texas Landowners Conservancy, and Extortion and the Ag Commissioner.

April 14, 2005

Extortion and the Ag Commissioner

In the San Antonio Express-News today, the GOP Texas Agricultural Commissioner, Susan Combs, continues the push by real estate developers to compel taxpayers to pay them not to pollute our water supplies.  The arguments are the same flawed ones that these alleged "property rights" advocates have been using for years, and that I wrote about last week when Combs and her co-conspirators, including the Texas Landowners Conservancy, a deceptive coalition of real estate developers and land speculators, introduced their bill in the Texas legislature.

Combs writes:

As environmental regulations in some parts of our state have grown more stringent over the past two decades or so, the definition of what constitutes "takings" has blurred. We are at a critical point where some types of environmental regulations are, in effect, takings because they cause dramatic devaluation of private property.

If such regulations are for the betterment of the general public, then the landowner who pays a dear price in loss of value should, under both state and federal constitutions, be compensated fairly.

Typically for "takings" advocates, Combs completely neglects all the benefits that rules about appropriate land use confer on property owners.  For instance, regulations to protect the environment serve to make the area a more desirable place to live and work and thus to increase property values.  To be consistent, Combs should advocate that landowners compensate governments and their taxpayers when such rules raise property values.

Combs wants developers to enjoy all the benefits of being part of a community, with none of the responsibility.   There are two potential scenarios if her bill becomes law:

  • Combs Case 1:  Cities regulate land use to make their community a desirable place for all and so boosting land values, yet are forced to pay developers huge amounts of money merely to behave as responsible citizens.  Developers thus enjoy a double benefit, while the community at large pays handsomely.
  • Combs Case 2:  Cities cannot afford land use regulations; developers subdivide, pave, and rake in the dough.  In the long term, water supplies become polluted, ugly development proliferates, quality of life decreases, and land values are depressed.  The developers have already run off with their money, while the community at large pays in all these indirect ways.

In both of these cases, developers and land speculators reap huge benefits at the expense of the general public and the taxpayers. 

John Echeverria of the Georgetown Enivronmental Law and Policy Institute points out some of the flaws in arguments such as Combs' in a 2001 article:

[I]n demanding public payment as a condition of refraining from harming our environment, takings advocates seek unfair windfalls at public expense. After all, reasonable environmental standards benefit everyone, including private property owners. In addition, much of the value of private property is created by nearby public investments, and it is hardly fair for owners to demand payments from the public based on speculative value created in part by the public in the first place.

This argument for the legitimacy of public regulation of the uses of private property suggests no disrespect for private property as an institution or its importance to the proper functioning of our economy. One of the key purposes of government is to safeguard property owners against theft and fraud. But the public's role in safeguarding private property rights goes hand in hand with the public's authority to ensure that those rights are exercised responsibly.

Fortunately, a majority of the current Supreme Court recognizes the beneficial aspect of environmental regulations, as Echeverria relates in an article about a 2002 Supreme Court decision about a development moratorium for the land surrouding Lake Tahoe:

The decision also expressly affirmed the validity of the principle that owners subject to regulatory restrictions enjoy a "reciprocity of advantage" with their neighbors.  While any particular owner may feel burdened by restrictions, she or he also receives implicit compensation from the neighbors' compliance with the same or similar restrictions.

In the Tahoe case, the court questioned whether land owners had suffered any actual economic loss; after all, the court reasoned, the moratorium provided the opportunity to prepare a comprehensive plan to protect the natural resources that made the basin a desirable place to invest in the first place, increasing property values for all.

How about this scenario as an alternative to the bleak Combs scenarios:

Echeverria Case 1:  Cities regulate land use to make their community a desirable place for all and so boosting land values, yet are forced to pay developers huge amounts of money merely to behave as responsible citizens.  Developers thus enjoy a double benefit, while the community at large pays handsomely.  Everyone, including developers and other landowners, benefits accordingly.
