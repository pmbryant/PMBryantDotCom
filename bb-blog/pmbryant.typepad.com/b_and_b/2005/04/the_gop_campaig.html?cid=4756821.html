April 05, 2005

The GOP campaign to stack the courts

One of our not-so-great Senators from the great state of Texas, John Cornyn, has furthered Tom DeLay's recent vague threats against the federal judiciary by airing his concerns that federal judges inspire violence against themselves with their "political" decisions:

In a Senate floor speech in which he sharply criticized a recent Supreme Court ruling on the death penalty, Cornyn (R-Tex.) -- a former Texas Supreme Court justice and member of the Judiciary Committee -- said Americans are growing increasingly frustrated by what he describes as activist jurists.

"It causes a lot of people, including me, great distress to see judges use the authority that they have been given to make raw political or ideological decisions," he said. Sometimes, he said, "the Supreme Court has taken on this role as a policymaker rather than an enforcer of political decisions made by elected representatives of the people."

Cornyn continued: "I don't know if there is a cause-and-effect connection, but we have seen some recent episodes of courthouse violence in this country. . . . And I wonder whether there may be some connection between the perception in some quarters, on some occasions, where judges are making political decisions yet are unaccountable to the public, that it builds up and builds up and builds up to the point where some people engage in, engage in violence. Certainly without any justification, but a concern that I have."

As Josh Marshall points out, Cornyn is a "wholly-owned subsidiary of the Bush White House."  These, and Tom DeLay's earlier threat, are obviously not off-the-cuff remarks. They are part of a long-term, calculated effort on the part of the GOP to delegitimize the federal judiciary in the eyes of their rabid partisans.

This campaign is obviously now ramping up.  The GOP and the Bush administration used the (sure-loser) Schiavo case to bring the judicial issue back into the limelight, and now they are ramping up the anti-judge rhetoric.  No doubt the ultimate goal is to make sure their base is completely up-in-arms when the time comes for the Senate GOP to push through Bush's extremist judicial nominations or the "nuclear option" of eliminating the traditional filibuster rules. The result would be an prolonged era of conservative judicial activism, with courts striking down, or severely curtailing, much of the popular, progressive legislation that has been enacted over the last seventy years— legislation that the congressional GOP is too politically craven to revoke directly. 

But why, if the GOP intends to advance their own judicial activism, are they engaged in a war of words against federal judges?  I belive it's because that's all it is—a mere war of words.  If they truly intended to reduce the power of the judicial branch, Congress, from what I've read, has much more direct and powerful means to do so, such as defunding courts and even impeaching judges.

Marshall Wittman at Bull Moose, who I have much respect for, suggests that "Democrats must renew their democratic spirit."  On the contrary, the Democratic Party's stand against the stacking of the courts with conservative extremists is a fight for the democratic spirit of the country.

Beyond the issue of the judiciary, it is sad, depressing, and downright dangerous that inhumane rhetoric like Cornyn's and Delay's is now an acceptable tactic on the part of leaders in the Republican party.  To make sure that it doesn't remain an acceptable tactic, we need to turn their words against them, and make sure that the sensible majority in this country realize what people like Cornyn and DeLay and others in the GOP are saying.  We cannot afford to allow these words to only get out to the right-wing base for whom they are aimed and to the progressive activists who are already aware.

My local paper, the San Antonio Express-News, does not appear to have any mention of Cornyn's statements today (at least in the online edition).  The first small steps, on my part, are to write them and ask them to cover this issue, and to ask for others to do the same for their own local media outlets.
