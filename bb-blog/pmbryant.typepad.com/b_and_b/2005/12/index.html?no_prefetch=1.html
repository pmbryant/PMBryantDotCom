So why are we going back to the Moon?

So NASA has plans to send humans back to the Moon in the next 15 years or so (pending funding, of course). Why do this? Isn't the Moon so 1960s?

Lunar scientist Paul Spudis comes to the rescue and tries to answer these questions in the Washington Post yesterday. How convincing is he?

The essence of his argument:

The moon is important for three reasons: science, inspiration and resources.

The scientific extravaganza of sending humans to the Moon, according to Spudis, consists of learning the history of our corner of the Solar System and using the Moon as a platform for radio astronomy.

Spudis has been studying the Moon for decades, so it would be understandable for him to play up the importance of the Moon to learning about the early history of our planetary neighborhood. The broader community of planetary scientists does not appear to agree, however, as there have been virtually no scientific missions sent to the Moon in the last three decades, while numerous ones have been sent to every other corner of the Solar System. If science at the Moon has not been worth sending unmanned robots, why is it worth sending humans on missions that have a significant risk to life and limb?

Is it for the inspirational value?

In 21st-century America, our existence depends on an educated, technically literate workforce, motivated and schooled in complex scientific disciplines. Tackling the challenges of creating a functioning society off-planet will require not only the best technical knowledge we can muster but also the best imaginations. One cannot develop a creative imagination, the renewable resource of a vibrant society, without confronting and surmounting unknowns and challenges on new frontiers.
Yet we have plenty of unknowns and "new frontier" challenges here on Earth, which is a far more fascinating, complicated, and lively place than the Moon. The idea that our society would be relegated to a creative desert without the challenge of doing something our grandfathers did is nonsensical—and insulting.

So perhaps it's all those great, valuable resources the Moon holds, just waiting to be tapped?

Water is an extremely valuable commodity in space -- in its liquid form, it supports human life, and it can be broken down into its two components, hydrogen and oxygen.
Indeed, if there's one thing where the Moon beats Earth, it's with water. The Moon, aka "Water World". Wait! The Earth already has water. In place, it's miles deep! Why in the world would we go to the Moon, where the "Sea of Tranquility" refers to a bone-dry lava bed, for water that may not even be there? Because, according to Spudis, we can use it to make fuel. Fuel for what?
The ability to make fuel on the moon will allow routine access to Earth-moon space, the zone in which all of our space assets reside.
Ah, so we would go to the Moon to make fuel for going to the Moon! Or to get back to Earth after going to the Moon. Or something like that.

But are there are other resources that may be useful?

Solar power, collected on the moon and beamed to Earth and throughout the space between the two, can provide a clean and reliable energy source not only for space-based applications but ultimately for users on Earth as well.
Clean and reliable energy for us stuck here on Earth. Sounds great! But is that realistic, or just a pipe dream?
Lunar solar power solves the apparent "showstopper" of other space-based solar power systems -- the high cost of getting the solar arrays into space. Instead of launching arrays from the deep gravity well of Earth, we would use the local soil and make hundreds of tons of solar panels on the moon.
This would be great if the infrastructure to make hundreds of tons of solar panels on the moon just magically appeared and didn't have to be launched from the deep gravity well of Earth itself.

Typical of boosters of NASA's new lunar vision, Spudis clearly feels there is an element of manifest destiny involved:

To become a multiplanet species, we must master the skills of extracting local resources, build our capability to journey and explore in hostile regions, and create new reservoirs of human culture and experience. That long journey begins on the moon -- the staging ground, supply station and classroom for our voyage into the universe.
Yet the justification for why becoming a "mutiplanet species" is necessary, feasable, or even desirable is, as we have seen, feeble.

Maybe there is indeed a reason to send humans back to the Moon—to avoid the national embarrassment of ending this country's experiment with manned space travel with the fizzing out of the troubled Space Shuttle program over the next few years. (In fact, Spudis himself was quoted to this effect in a September article: "[I]t’s better than the alternative, which is extinction of human exploration.")

Perhaps that reason fits in Spudis' "inspirational" category. But I disagree. Whatever its other shortcomings, a goal of sending humans to a far more fascinating and challenging destination—the planet Mars—would be inspirational. Returning to an airless, waterless, lifeless world first visited nearly two generations ago is not.

Intelligent design floundering

The anti-evolution forces that are attempting to insert the teaching of "Intelligent Design" into public school science classes suffered a resounding defeat two days ago, as has been widely reported.

No doubt, there will be plenty of further action on this front in the future, as much of the public remains unconvinced of the scientific robustness of evolutionary theory. And so, as biologist P.Z. Myers of Pharyngula points out, scientists need to improve at presenting their case to the public. But, despite the media hype in recent months, the evidence is very thin that the anti-evolution forces are anywhere close to overturning our society's collective judgement that real science, not religious pseudo-science, belongs in public school science classes.

After all, last month, in the small, conservative, semi-rural town of Dover, Pennsylvania, voters narrowly, but decisively, rejected virtually every member of the ID-supporting school board.

And now a federal judge appointed by none other than George W. Bush has given the ID movement a very public smackdown.

Those are the signs, not of a vigorous movement, but of a floundering one.

Arctic refuge given reprieve from GOP plunder

A mixture of bad news and absence of bad news from our corruptly-led Congress today: the regressive budget bill passed by the slimmest of margins (no doubt by design), yet the GOP's despicable tactic of attaching the extremely controversial proposal to drill for oil in the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge to the defense appropriations bill failed thanks to a filibuster by Democrats and a pair of Republicans.

Mike Thomas summarizes the budget bill at his blog, Rhetoric and Rhythm.

On the measure to drill in the Arctic Refuge, the Washington Post writes:

In debate before the cloture vote, [Senator Ted Stevens, R-Alaska] told fellow senators, "We know this Arctic. You don't know the Arctic at all." At issue, he said, was "2,000 acres of the Arctic," the amount of land in the refuge that would be opened for drilling. "Is that worth this fight?"

...

Sen. Joseph I. Lieberman (D-Conn.) said that even though the bill provides vital defense funding, he was joining the filibuster on principle to prevent attachments such as the drilling provision. "If we yield to this tactic on ANWR," he said, "next year it will be someone else's pet project attached to the defense spending bill."

Stevens has trashed any remaining integrity he may have had with his actions in recent days, essentially holding funds for our troops and for relief for Katrina victims hostage to his pet project.

Kudos to Senator Lieberman for standing up to this blackmail and helping to defeat it.

Shame on Senator John McCain, who just yesterday talked a good game about the legislative tactics of Stevens and others ("Our system is broken if our national security is at stake and we carry on spending for the special interests as if nothing were wrong"), but who was unwilling to do anything about it.

UPDATE: Matt at Just Another Blog has more on the Arctic Refuge drilling vote.

December 19, 2005

March of the Pelicans

Back in November, the B and B team, along with a couple friends and fellow bird watchers, visited what is often considered to be the best birding spot in the San Antonio area: a former wastewater treatment plant, current wildlife refuge, called Mitchell Lake.

Indeed, with our friends' help, we were able to identify over forty-five different species of birds that afternoon. Now I don't keep formal lists or anything resembling them, but still, this was, by far, a record for me for a single day. The species we saw included many life birds for me, such as stunning Northern Pintails, wacky Hooded Mergansers, and cute Buffleheads. So it was quite thrilling.

Until a year or so ago, Mitchell Lake was run by the San Antonio Water System (SAWS) and closed to the public except for guided tours given by San Antonio Audubon volunteers on occasional Saturday mornings. Given the long drive from our house and the early hour of the walks, we never were able to make it to one of those events. Now, though, the National Audubon Society manages the place for SAWS and opens it to the public all day on Saturdays. So last winter, our two visits were our first experience with this bird wonderland.

It was on one of these visits a year ago that I experienced one of my most embarrassing birding moments.

Given Mitchell Lake's obscurity with the general public, but great reputation with local birders, I suspect that roughly 100% of its visitors are more knowledgeable about birds than I am. So I don't consider myself a good judge of what birds are especially interesting to them. (After all, I just added the relatively common Pintails, Mergansers, and Buffleheads to my non-existent life list a month ago.) Nonetheless, when we passed a few other people on one of the trails after spending a couple hours inspecting the place, and they asked if we had seen anything of note, I felt confident enough to offer up a response. I don't remember my exact words, but I do remember pointing up at the graceful, giant white birds soaring in the distance, saying, "And those penguins, of course!"

The quizzical look I received in response eventually got me to realize what I had said, but only after I had finished my brief discourse. We all had a good laugh at the concept of penguins flying high over the countryside south of San Antonio. And forevermore the other half of the B and B team has teased me about this slip of the tongue.

For instance, that recent, popular documentary about the birds that march dozens of miles across the Antarctic wasteland to hatch their chicks: that movie is known as "March of the Pelicans" around here.

Congress' public land sell-off killed: "A welcome stay of execution"

A small bit of good news from the U.S. Congress. Of course, in this age of corrupt Republican leadership, good news means a smaller amount of bad news than there would otherwise be. The bad news that we are now without is the GOP's sneak-attack on our public lands.

"It's a welcome stay of execution."
So says the Superintendent of Death Valley National Park, J.T. Reynolds, as quoted in the Los Angeles Times today. The details:
Republicans in Congress late Tuesday stripped proposed mining law revisions from a budget bill that critics said could have led to the sell-off of millions of acres of federal land, including portions of national parks and forests, such as Death Valley National Park and Mojave National Preserve.

The package faced mounting bipartisan opposition from Western senators, whose support was crucial, after scores of groups, including a coalition of hunting and fishing interests, complained. A Senate spokesman said opposition to the mining law revisions could have jeopardized passage of the budget bill.

In an interview with The Times, the author of the proposals, Rep. Jim Gibbons (R-Nev.), denied that criticism from park officials, hunting and fishing groups, and others had led to the decision. He said the furor was the result of "intentionally false and misleading information put out by anti-mining groups … that had no impact on the fact that we are here today."

Gibbons vowed to reintroduce what he called comprehensive mining reform legislation in the new year.

We already know about the massive criticism of the Pombo-Gibbons provision in the budget reconciliation bill, including the wishy-washy opposition of a handful of western Republicans. But what was the final straw? I suspect it may have been this:
On Tuesday, a coalition of more than two dozen hunting and fishing groups claiming to represent 55 million hunters and fishermen sent a letter to Rep. Jim Nussle, an Iowa Republican who chairs the House Budget Committee, and Rep. John M. Spratt Jr., a South Carolina Democrat and the committee's ranking minority member, expressing "serious concerns."

"America's hunters and anglers depend upon public lands and waters … to pursue their tradition of hunting and fishing," the groups wrote. "This proposal to sell public land is being universally poorly received throughout the hunting and angling community."

Thanks to all who worked to kill this horrible idea. Great job.

Now on to killing the next bad idea, of which there are far, far too many coming from our so-called representatives in the House.

So the temporary lack of this one piece of bad news is hardly reason to celebrate. Instead, it is well past time to throw these corrupt corporate elitists out on their ears and replace them with individuals who have the interests of the honest people of this country at heart.

GOP's public lands sale "stinks out loud"

More voices in opposition to the GOP's Pombo-Gibbons provision in the federal budget resolution that would authorize the sell-off of large areas of our nation's public lands (as previously written about here and here):

  • Trout Unlimited: "Pombo’s fire-sale of public lands would reverse a 30-year congressional mandate that public lands remain in public ownership. As more private lands are subdivided or posted, public lands provide the last access for sportsmen who wish to fish, hunt, and camp with their families and friends. That access would be effectively lost as a result of Pombo’s legislation."

  • Charlie Kerr, president of the board of directors of the Western Colorado Congress: "I don’t think I’ve seen anything worse than the potential of this bill to basically privatize our heritage."

  • Oregon Rep. Peter DeFazio (D): "A lot of people would be out buying survey stakes and flagging if this bill passes. It might be kind of like the old days. Maybe they'll make everybody line up and then rush for the land. ... You could go into the middle of the Willamette National Forest and stake a claim. All you have to do is purport that it has mineral value, then once you purchased it, you could do with it what you wish."

  • Montrose County (Colorado) Commissioner Allan Belt, former director of the Bureau of Land Management’s Uncompahgre Field Office: "I think the bill stinks out loud. You’re going to find a vast army of people coming out against this."

  • Chris Wood, vice president of Trout Unlimited: "Our membership is up in arms over this. We've gotten more response on this issue than any other from our general membership."

  • Jon Marvel, executive director of the Western Watersheds Project: "This is the largest land grab in the West since the opening of Indian Territory in Oklahoma in 1902."

  • Sen. Pete Domenici and Rep. Heather Wilson (R), both of New Mexico:"If enacted, this bill could lead to a rapid sale of public lands throughout the West."

  • Palm Beach Post, editorial: "America is not so cash-strapped and morally bankrupt that it must sell lands in national parks and at national monuments to balance the budget just because one [sic] lawmaker with no environmental conscience happens to think that it's a good idea."

  • Bob Abbey, former Bureau of Land Management state director for Nevada: "There is nothing positive about this bill unless you happen to have ownership in a mining company. ... Having spent half my life managing the public's land and being a proponent of responsible mining, I assure you this legislation is bad for American taxpayers."

  • Elyssa Rosen, a senior policy adviser for the Great Basin Mine Watch: "It [Bob Abbey's position] just shows you that most of us - even folks who have staked out different positions over the years on how to manage public lands - are now converging in opposition to this bill."

  • Tucson resident Mary Kidwell: "This is a loss for Arizonans. We could soon find 'private property' signs blocking the trailhead of favorite hunting grounds or picnic spots."

  • Former Montana congressman Pat Williams (D): "We westerners don’t want our land put up for grabs and we resent this midnight, slam-dunk attempt to change the federal law to encourage the sale of our birthright."

  • Hal Rothman, political analyst and history professor with the University of Nevada, Las Vegas: "This is a sloppy and poorly prepared bill that is not thought out very well and has more to do with grandstanding than protecting American land."

  • San Juan County (Colorado) administrator Willy Tookey: "We've been promoting development, but also trying to protect our public lands. If all of a sudden public land becomes private and 'no trespassing' signs go up everywhere, sure, we'd have a slightly bigger tax base, but that could be terrible for our recreation and tourism economy."

  • Auden Schendler, director of environmental affairs for Aspen Skiing Co.: "Our concern is that this would threaten the natural beauty and open view sheds that make Colorado's tourism and outdoor ... economy so vibrant."

  • Colorado Sen. Ken Salazar (D): "Part of what makes Colorado special is its natural heritage that includes outdoor recreation. To degrade those opportunities with this unprecedented assault on public lands is something about which every Coloradan should be concerned."

  • Idaho Sen. Larry Craig (R): "Mining law and its reform should be used for producing the metals and minerals for our industrial base and that alone."

  • Mining-claims dealer Kent Taylor: "That would be the total most-retarded thing they could do. I just could see a floodgate of scams opening. Somebody's going to be lining their pockets big time."

  • Montana Gov. Brian Schweitzer (D): "If a skunk comes into the house, you can throw it in the shower and clean it with soap, but it’s still going to smell bad. You will not get the stink off this one with a little shower and soap.’’
In the face of this withering criticism over the last couple weeks, Gibbons today made a slight change to his and Pombo's smelly provision:
Nevada Rep. Jim Gibbons, R-Nev., author of the mining legislation, said Monday he would remove language from the bill that would have allowed the direct sale of some lands that no longer contained minerals.
In Governor Schweitzer's words, this brief shower isn't gong to get the stink off.

December 09, 2005

Opposition to GOP's public lands sell-off builds

The sneaking of a provision to sell off our nation's public lands into a recently-passed budget bill—courtesy the GOP leadership in the persons of Rep. Richard Pombo and Rep. Jim Gibbons—is attracting more and more negative attention—from Democrats and Republicans and others in between.

The L.A. Times published a reminder of the issues at stake earlier this week:

Standing at the foot of billion-year-old Stripe Mountain, acting park chief [of Mojave National Preserve in California] Larry Whalon gazed up at ancient slopes banded in limestone and copper.

"In 10 years, there could be a big house right here. Lots of houses," Whalon said.

The entire mountain in the desert preserve west of Las Vegas is covered by federal mining claims, and newly proposed legislation would allow claim holders to purchase this land outright.

This is not going down well with many governors of western states, where most of the affected public land lies:

Six western governors put Congress on notice this week they oppose a measure pending in the U.S. Senate that would open millions of acres of public lands for sale to mining companies.

Wyoming Gov. Dave Freudenthal wrote Sens. Judd Gregg, R-N.H., and Kent Conrad, D-N.D., of the Senate Budget Committee, on Thursday saying the sale of public lands in a budget reconciliation bill passed by the House could cut off public access for recreation and deny the states billions of dollars in mineral royalties.

...

The letter was signed by Govs. Brian Schweitzer of Montana, Janet Napolitano of Arizona, Bill Richardson of New Mexico, Ted Kulongoski of Oregon, and Christine Gregoire of Washington.

Note that all of these six western governors are Democrats.

Of course, some Republicans are not exactly thrilled about Pombo's and Gibbons' efforts either, given the huge outcry that has come from many hunters and fisherman who frequently vote Republican. But their comments have been quite timid.

[Colorado Sen. Wayne] Allard ... said in a release Tuesday that it's worth considering reforms to the mining law, but not in the middle of the budget debate.

"Concerns about the implications of this provision have been raised by public interest groups and private property groups," Allard said. "I look forward to working with my colleagues to update the 1872 mining law, but I do not believe the provision under consideration by the conference committee is the way to achieve this needed reform of the law."

Can a statement be much more half-hearted?

In contrast, we have this:

Outrage over the bill has risen to fever pitch in Montana, where U.S. Sen. Max Baucus, a Democrat, is leading the call to squelch the mining measure. "It's an abominable idea," he said in an interview Tuesday. "Our public lands are for the public.''

Backers of the measure say it would authorize at most 360,000 acres for sale, funneling roughly $158 million into depleted federal coffers. Opponents say wording in the bill opens for sale millions of acres across the West, including some of the West's most prized landscapes.

A case in point in western Montana is Rock Creek, a blue-ribbon trout stream that draws tens of thousands each year to recreate in an area made famous for its abundance of cutthroat, elk, bighorn sheep and mountain goats.

The recreation site also is home to more than 3,700 mining claims, all of which could be sold, if the measure becomes law.

Walter Lore, who lives near Rock Creek and has been fishing and hunting there for years, said closing access to any part of the area would make Montanans "big-time upset. We might have a civil war."

The Pombo-Gibbons provision is so bad, that even industry-friendly former government officials are willing to say so—and not in wishy-washy Allard words:

The former top U.S. land manager for the biggest gold mining state in the nation says a proposal in Congress to privatize public mining lands "would be catastrophic, both environmentally and economically."

Bob Abbey, considered an ally of the mining industry before his retirement in June as the Bureau of Land Management's state director for Nevada, said the proposal by Republican Reps. Richard Pombo of California and Jim Gibbons of Nevada offers false hopes to rural communities to attract new businesses.

"There is nothing positive about this bill unless you happen to have ownership in a mining company," Abbey said.

"Having spent half my life managing the public's land and being a proponent of responsible mining, I assure you this legislation is bad for American taxpayers," he said in a letter first published Thursday in the Reno Gazette-Journal.

It's time for Pombo-Gibbons, and the Republican leadership's efforts to privatize our public lands for the benefit of their financial backers, to be tossed in the slag heap of history.
