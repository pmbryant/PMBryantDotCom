August 25, 2005

Big Bend Ranch will remain whole

As I suspected, once exposed to public scrutiny, the proposed sell-off of a large portion of one of the Big Bend Ranch State Park, has been defeated. The Austin American-Statesman reports

Texas Parks and Wildlife Department commissioners Thursday unanimously rejected a ranch owner's bid to buy more than 46,000 acres of Big Bend Ranch State Park in Presido County.

Park and wilderness activists, conservation group representatives and even former department employees opposed to the land sale broke into applause with the vote. The land under consideration which would have included archeological sites and creek habitat that is home to threatened and endangered animals.

The vote closed two contentious days of testimony about losing a part of the state's largest park and the lack of public knowledge of the proposal until the Austin American-Statesman disclosed the proposed sale this week.

...

Few people who testified before the commission Wednesday and Thursday agreed with the department's proposal to sell. Many criticized the lack of extensive public input on the state's divesting any portion of the park.

"The area in question contains 14 recorded pristine archeological sites, and at least 100 additional sites are known," said Larry Oaks, executive director of the Texas Historical Commission. "These sites range from prehistoric rock art to rock shelters and ruins of early historic settlements. There are remnants of Late Prehistoric Cielo Complex dwellings native only to this part of Texas."

Oaks said he was appalled by the lack of discussion of the sale before this week.

"It appears not only did you not consult us about this proposed sale, but (you also) didn't consult early with members of your own staff. . . . We are strongly opposed to the sale of any parcel of this pristine area before further study can be conducted."

[Houston businessman and prospective land-purchaser John] Poindexter had agreed to a state-mandated conservation easement on the 46,000 acres. The easement would have been designed to protect creekside habitat along Cienega Creek, the only creek at Big Bend Ranch that has fish. The creek originates on Cibolo Creek Ranch, Poindexter's adjacent resort.

However, the easement would have allowed development of 4,600 acres, including some of the highly prized land along and overlooking the creek. Park activists argued that the department was giving away too much of its control of the Cienega region of the ranch, especially the water, without accepting public input.

...

One issue that kept cropping up was whether the department can protect water in Cienega Creek. Since the water that makes the creek originates on Poindexter's ranch, there is some question about whether he might capture the water there and reduce or even stop the flow into the park.

"It is mine to do with as I wish," Poindexter said. "It will be used sooner or later by myself or my successors."

Is that last line a threat? I would hope that water law in this state would not allow such behavior. But water law can be very convoluted and primitive, so I can't say.

Thanks to all who helped defeat this proposal on such short notice.

August 24, 2005

Texas to sell off "very special place" in Big Bend to resort owner?

For Texans who care about public lands and open government, this story is something to keep a very close eye on:

Texas may sell part of Big Bend Ranch State Park
Associated Press

AUSTIN - State park officials are considering selling one-sixth of the flagship Big Bend Ranch State Park, the Austin American-Statesman reported today.

Texas Parks and Wildlife commissioners are to discuss the sale of 45,000 acres of the 300,000-acre park in a closed session Wednesday in Austin. The first public discussion and a vote by the commission could come Thursday.

Houston businessman John Poindexter confirmed he has been negotiating with the Parks and Wildlife Department. Poindexter, who owns the exclusive resort Cibolo Creek Ranch adjacent to the park, had no further comment.

Word of the talks has drawn criticism from a former parks commissioner who decried the possible sale and from an environmental advocate who called the secret preparations for a sale bad public policy.

Parks and Wildlife Executive Director Bob Cook said Monday that selling the odd-shaped land that juts out from the rest of the park could help the department square up the park's boundaries and simplify management of the vast desert site in West Texas.

...

Former Parks and Wildlife commissioner Bob Armstrong, who worked to have the state acquire the Big Bend property and protect it as a park, views the proposal with skepticism. Armstrong also is a former state land commissioner and former legislator.

"To trade this off would be a shame," Armstrong said.

The area has a spring known as the Cienega, and that has value beyond any money the department might acquire, Armstrong said.

"That's a very special place," he said.

Armstrong said the stovepipe shape of the northwest corner of the ranch was laid out in order to preserve the springs and creeks that make up the area.

Ken Kramer of the Sierra Club said what bothers him most is the secrecy surrounding the possible sale.

"To have something like this happen without public discussion is bad public policy," he said.

[Parks and Wildlife Executive Director] Cook said the parks department wasn't trying to hide the sale, only to protect the ongoing negotiations.

It's not like we in Texas already have such a huge surplus of public lands that we should be selling off 45,000 acres of a "very special place" to a resort owner with the excuse of using the money to purchase 25,000 acres of inholdings. In this huge state, the total area of managed parklands and wildlife management areas is only 1.3 million acres. This proposed sale is several percent of that!

And the excuse for the secrecy doesn't fly—not if the Parks Director has the public interest at heart, at least. Bring this out into the light of day, and we'll see if it survives the exposure.

August 04, 2005

'I and the Bird' #3: The Backyard and Beyond

Welcome to the third edition of the blog carnival for bird lovers, I and the Bird. It is a great honor for me to host this, especially since, while I am indeed a bird lover, I am not a particularly skillful bird watcher. But I enjoy birds greatly. Yes, even those I do not know exactly what they may be called because they don't seem to match up with anything in my field guide. And even those that I can't get a decent look at because they're flitting about in that dense brush so close, yet so far. Finally, after many years at it, I think I have a good understanding of which species birds live in and around our backyard and even know a fair amount of their behavior. It's been a slow process.

Take me far out of this immediate area, however, and I am back to being a beginner. Of course, the great thing about that is that there's always new birds to see, and new bird behavior to learn.

In addition to watching birds, I also enjoy reading about the experiences of other bird lovers, from those who are occasional indulgers in birding like myself, to those who are expert amateurs, to those who are professionals. The previous editions of "I and the Bird" have been a great way to discover such writings. The submissions for this new edition follow in the excellent, if short, tradition of the first two.

This birding carnival, in the way that I am comfortable with, starts out very close to home.

  • Aydin Örstan at Snail's Tales writes about how he used a couple common backyard birds who showed up on his deck to benchmark his hearing, in 'The Cardinals at the limit of my hearing.'

  • Nuthatch at bootstrap analysis, expounds upon the habits of a small, gregarious bird that accompanies her in the garden throughout the summer in 'an exuberant bird in a plain brown wrapper.'

  • Yours truly wrote about my experience last year at finally, and unexpectedly, sighting an elusive bird whose nighttime call has been a comforting presence every spring for the last six years.

  • Clare Kines at The House & other Arctic musings tells us about the different types of seagulls that inhabit his part of the world. Read 'Gulls', so that next time you spot a seagull, you can say, "Look! A glaucous gull."

  • Mike at 10,000 Birds reports on a birding excursion away from home, but in a part of the world he visits regularly in order to burn a giant chicken. Or something like that. Read 'Birds Of Chicken Inferno 2005 for a full explanation—and also to see a picture of the "gigantic galliform" itself.
Moving on, the carnival travels into the domain of surveys. I have some experience with this, having participated in a few bird surveys myself, as well as the Great Backyard Bird Count.
  • Unlike Duncan at Ben Cruachan Blog, however, I do not have experience with parrots. (Except for that recent charming movie about the guy in San Francisco.) Duncan writes about his parrot (and other bird) counting experience along the southern coast of Australia in 'Parrot arithmetic.'

  • Tony G at milkriverblog, just up the road here in central Texas, reports on the results of a recent, and rather unique, survey of his own in Wakeup Calls.
Next, the carnival moves into the quest for rarities, a popular pursuit amongst many birders, but one I have yet to try out myself.
  • John at A DC Birding Blog sets out on a local expedition to spot two reported species not normally found in his environs this time of year. To find out what he was looking for and whether he found it, read 'A Sultry Evening Twitch.'

  • A British blogger at bogbumper reports on a couple of rare, spectacular birds in Britain that attracted quite a crowd before an unfortunate ending. For details, read 'The day the Bee-eaters got eaten.'
What would a bird carnival excursion be without the chance to see a hawk eat a meal? (Not the bee-eaters, fortunately!) Today we have double the fun.
  • First, the illustrated version, including action shots, from Rexroth's Daughter at Dharma Bums, who has a whole sequence of pictures in her post 'A Hawk Gets Lunch.'

  • Next, the narrated version from Birdchick at Birdchick Blog. As she describes in First Blood at the Bird Store, this particular hawk may have been a novice at his work.
After that lunch break, it's time to get up-close and personal with the next group of birds.
  • Dave at the Bird Treatment and Learning Center in Anchorage, Alaska relates the story of a hero of a bird in 'Quality Time with One Wing & Ol' Witch'. It is about his encounter with an eagle whose will to live not only helped him survive devastating injuries suffered in the infamous Exxon Valdez oil spill, but also helped save the lives of numerous other eagles also injured in the spill.

  • Jason at Beakspeak treats us with some spectacular shots of hummingbird babies growing up in his posts The Hummingbird Chronicles and The Hummingbird Chronicles: An Update. He even got a short video of one as it was on the verge of learning to fly. Don't miss it!
Finally, we get to explore the more personal side of birding.
  • Charlie Moores at Charlie's Bird Blog explores the question of why he devotes much of his scarce spare time to watching these small, flying creatures in a post entitled 'How in a rambling conversation I get to see why I go birding'. (There is no link directly to the post, so if you encounter this at some future date, go to his blog and either scroll down or look in his archives.)
And so the third edition of 'I and the Bird,' with all its multi-media glory, comes to a close. Yet the fourth edition, which will be hosted at milkriverblog, is only two weeks away. Please send your submissions to either Mike at 10,000 Birds or Tony G at milkriverblog on or before Tuesday, August 16. I am looking forward to the next edition already!

August 03, 2005

A loud, elusive, comforting bird

Every spring, each day as the sun drops below the horizon, the call starts. A low, soft, clicking sound, rapidly followed by a loud multi-pitched, three-part whistle. It is unmistakeable. It is the sign of the chuck-wills-widow—the odd name of the bird derived from its distinctive call.

We've heard that bird—sometimes more than one—for several months every night of every spring in each of the six springs we have lived in our current house. As the sky gets dark, the call starts out, seemingly tentative, at relatively low volume and infrequent intervals. As darkness creeps in, the call gets more insistent, growing louder, repeating every few seconds. Someone clearly wants to be heard. And this goes on for hours. Occasionally I would emerge outside in the middle of the night only to hear the bird still repeating that call endlessly. As the Texas summer approaches, the call comes less frequently until at some point it ceases. I do not know exactly when that is, as in the Texas summer, we are not out in the backyard so often. After all, it's kind of hot here then.

Despite all this commotion over all these years, I have never seen the bird. I know from field guides that it is not a small creature, as it is larger than the scrub jays that frequent our yard during the daytime, occasionally scaring off the smaller songbirds. But the chuck-wills-widow is exquisitely camouflaged. During the daytime, you would practically have to step on one to see it, I suspect. And at night, when it is active, well, it's dark.

Ok, it's not quite true that I have never seen the bird. I did see it once, albeit not well. In the spring, a year ago.

A couple years earlier I had gotten curious one night and tried to fight through the cedar-covered lots surrounding our house to find out approximately where this bird lived. But after pushing through a few dozen feet, I realized the call wasn't getting appreciably louder. Clearly, this bird, which sounded so close, was not quite as close as I thought. So I gave up, not too disappointed since I found its constant presence comforting and didn't want to disturb it.

But then that day a bit over year ago, the call was literally booming in my ears one night. The sun had set perhaps 45 minutes earlier, but there was still a small amount of light in the sky. After deafening me for a short while, the call would stop for a minute, then resume booming in my ears, but from a bit further to the right. Now, he really had to be close. It just couldn't sound this loud and not be within a few dozen feet. Or could it? It had already fooled me once. So I decided to try to find out, by inching around the yard extremely slowly.

I had no hope of seeing anything. There was barely any light left. I merely hoped to triangulate the sound to estimate where the bird was located. Even this would be difficult, though, since, unusually, the sound of the call was moving around frequently—every minute or two. For several minutes I inched around, changed directions to follow the sound, inched around some more, and so on. Then, with the bird temporarily silent, I emerged into a clearing at the edge of a small dirt road about 100 feet behind our house. Then the call started again. Louder than ever. So ear-splittingly loud that I knew it had to be directly across the road from me, barely 10 feet away.

I froze. Finally, I believed I knew where the bird was, even if I couldn't see it. For the first time in four years of listening to that chuck-wills-widow, I could say that. And I must say, the volume of that bird's call was extremely impressive. I had never fully appreciated that before.

But my luck was about to get even better, for after a minute or so, as was its pattern that day, the bird decided to spare my ears and move further away. When it did so, as it took off, it emerged into a 3-foot wide trail cut through the dense cedar and I saw the bird's silhouette against the sky ever so briefly before it disappeared into the darkness.

I had not expected to ever see that bird. But it had briefly revealed itself to me. And I was thrilled.
