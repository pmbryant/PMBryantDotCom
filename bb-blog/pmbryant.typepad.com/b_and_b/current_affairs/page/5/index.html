October 18, 2004

Proud member of the Reality-Based Community

From political writer Matthew Yglesias:

One of the odd advantages that George W. Bush's nonsensical manner of running the government has provided is that it opens his administration up to so many different lines of attack that it's very hard for the anti-Bush forces to form a coherent critique. You've got Jim Henley and Marshall Whittman who agree about approximately nothing, joined in their opposition to Bush. I don't agree with either of them about much, but I certainly agree about the opposition to Bush thing. But now that shrill, Bush-hating libertarian Gene Healy is suggesting "Reality-Based Community" T-shirts it appears that the anti-Bush coalition may at last, thanks to an anonymous White House advisor, have found a unifying theme. We are not merely the Ancient and Hermetic Order of the Shrill, which may turn off some of your more mild-mannered Bush opponents, we are, proud members of the Reality-Based Community. On the other side is, well, the other guys.

Just in case you any of my few readers aren't aware of the source for the phrase "Reality-Based Community", it is from a feature article by Ron Suskind in Sunday's New York Times Magazine:

In the summer of 2002, after I had written an article in Esquire that the White House didn't like about Bush's former communications director, Karen Hughes, I had a meeting with a senior adviser to Bush. He expressed the White House's displeasure, and then he told me something that at the time I didn't fully comprehend -- but which I now believe gets to the very heart of the Bush presidency.

The aide said that guys like me were ''in what we call the reality-based community,'' which he defined as people who ''believe that solutions emerge from your judicious study of discernible reality.'' I nodded and murmured something about enlightenment principles and empiricism. He cut me off. ''That's not the way the world really works anymore,'' he continued. ''We're an empire now, and when we act, we create our own reality. And while you're studying that reality -- judiciously, as you will -- we'll act again, creating other new realities, which you can study too, and that's how things will sort out. We're history's actors . . . and you, all of you, will be left to just study what we do.''

I see that the esteemed progressive blogger Atrios has already signed up as a "Proud memeber of the Reality-Based Community". Count me in, too.

October 15, 2004

At least one journalist is waking up

Will Saletan, a middle-of-the-road political writer at Slate magazine, has slowly been waking up to Bush's lies. Now he writes:

I know I've been hard on the president lately. I'd like to say something nice about him. I'd like to be "fair and balanced." But my first responsibility as a reporter is to the truth. When one candidate tells half the truth, and the other says the truth doesn't matter, it becomes irresponsible for me or any other journalist not to report that by that standard—the standard of respecting the truth standard—one candidate is head and shoulders above the other.

Three cheers for Saletan.

Back in the late 1990s, when Slate was my favorite source for political news, Will Saletan was one of my favorite political writers. He was especially good during the Republican impeachment witch hunt against Clinton. Alas, during the first half Bush administration he backslid tremendously, became was too quick to forgive Bush's heinous acts, and even declared himself to be a Republican. I gave up reading him.

But after this article, plus his great series during the Republican Convention, I think I am going to resume reading him more regularly. We need more journalists willing to give us straight stories, without trying themselves in knots by trying to be "fair and balanced" to a bunch of flagrant liars.

October 13, 2004

GOP funding fraudulent voter registration practices

These debates have been going so well for Kerry that I am sorry that there won't be another one after tonight. It has also been enjoyable trekking into town to watch them at the local campaign headquarters with dozens of fellow Democrats.

Now the focus shifts to the get-out-the-vote efforts. Apparently the GOP is resorting to all sorts of dirty, even illegal, tricks to depress the Democratic vote. One of the most egregious examples was just uncovered by a Las Vegas investigative reporter in Nevada (emphasis added).

The out-of-state firm has been in Las Vegas for the past few months, registering voters. It employed up to 300 part-time workers and collected hundreds of registrations per day, but former employees of the company say that Voters Outreach of America only wanted Republican registrations.

Two former workers say they personally witnessed company supervisors rip up and trash registration forms signed by Democrats.

"We caught her taking Democrats out of my pile, handed them to her assistant and he ripped them up right in front of us. I grabbed some of them out of the garbage and she tells her assisatnt to get those from me," said Eric Russell, former Voters Outreach employee.

Eric Russell managed to retrieve a pile of shredded paperwork including signed voter registration forms, all from Democrats. We took them to the Clark County Election Department and confirmed that they had not, in fact, been filed with the county as required by law.

Unfortunately, from some digging by a reader of Daily Kos, it appears that this company has been up to the same fraudulent tactics in many other states. And also, that this company is funded by the Republican National Committee.

If this isn't voter fraud, I have a hard time imagining what would be.

October 05, 2004

Bull Moose endorses John Kerry

This may not mean much at first glance to the few of you who may read this, but the Bull Moose has endorsed John Kerry for President.

Back in the dark political days of early 2001, when political blogs were few and far between and Bush's appointment to power was still fresh in everybody's mind, Josh Marshall's Talking Points Memo and Marshall Wittman's Bull Moose were the only blogs I read regularly. Talking Points Memo to get the liberal-leaning anti-Bush point-of-view, and Bull Moose to hear from a conservative-leaner who clearly was not as enamored of the newly-appointed Bush as most of the others of his ilk.

Alas, much to my disappointment, Bull Moose shut down after only a year or so, as Wittman joined the staff of Senator John McCain, and I have yet to find another conservative-leaning writer that I could respect anywhere close to as much.

Anyway, after more than two years in the blog shadows, "Bull Moose" Wittman has emerged, and, lo and behold, is endorsing John Kerry for President:

This unreconstructed Bull Moose will run with the donkey in November.

I am an independent McCainiac who hopes to revive the Bull Moose tradition of Theodore Roosevelt, and I support the Kerry-Edwards agenda. Don't get me wrong -- this Bull Moose is not completely in agreement with the Democratic donkey. But the Bush administration has betrayed the effort to create a new politics of national greatness in the aftermath of 9/11.

If John Kerry wins, it remains to be seen whether his administration will be more willing to break with its ideological base than a Bush team that has been slavishly loyal to its corporate paymasters. But there is no remaining shred of doubt that another four years of a Bush presidency would have a toxic effect on American politics. If George W. Bush is re-elected, unlimited corporate power, cynicism, and division will ride high in the saddle.

...

If Kerry is victorious, there will no doubt be a battle within the Democratic Party between the left and New Democrat wings. Perhaps, just perhaps, a progressive national greatness wing can emerge that combines a commitment to national service and progressive economics with a dedication to defending America and promoting its ideals. Fortunately, there is a model for progressive national greatness in the presidency -- the previous JFK. President Kennedy combined a muscular foreign policy with a call to service and a domestic progressivism. Kennedy brought Republicans into his administration and governed from the vital center.

A President Kerry also should embrace a reform agenda that will attract the constituencies of McCain and H. Ross Perot. An overhaul of the tax system that would eliminate loopholes should be undertaken. A few years ago, Rep. Dick Gephardt offered a modified flat tax proposal that would both simplify the system and retain progressivity. A left-right coalition to eliminate corporate welfare should be built. A Kerry administration should promote efforts at the state level to depoliticize congressional redistricting.

...

Bush and Vice President Dick Cheney have waged an unprecedentedly cynical and divisive campaign. The campaign has proven that there are no guard rails when it comes to a scorched-earth effort to hold on to power. However, Democrats can seize the opportunity to reach out to disaffected moderate Republicans and independents to build a new political coalition of national unity. That is both the hope and the cause of this unreconstructed Bull Moose.

Remembering back to when I was a regular reader of his, I cannot claim to be shocked. Pleasantly surprised, though. I know I disagree with Wittman about numerous issues, but given my past respect for his writings, it is good to know that, on the big picture, at this time, we are on the same side.

October 01, 2004

Bush/Orwell 2004

I should wear my Kerry/Edwards button more often.

We went to watch the debate last night at the local Kerry campaign headquarters. I finally decided to wear my new Kerry/Edwards button. There was quite a crowd and we all thoroughly enjoyed Kerry's great performance.

Afterwards, we went out to eat at a local Mexican restaurant, Guajillo's (fabulous food, by the way). I forgot that I was still wearing the button, but I got nothing but positive reactions to it. The most interesting from from the manager of the place, closing up shop as I paid for our food. He saw my button and went on a rant about how he had put up a couple large "Bush/Orwell" signs: "Nobody knew who Orwell was," he cried in disgust. "They vote for Bush but nobody knows Orwell! They're ignorant. You really have to be ignorant to vote for Bush."

A good end to a good night.

Debate Matters

Fox News, of all the possible choices, will apparently be in control of the video cameras at the Presidential debate tonight, as I've learned via Josh Marshall. We are playing on a lop-sided field.

As always, beware the post-debate spin. For example, Marshall also relates that MSNBC had plans to put a hard-core Republican operative, Frank Luntz, in charge of their debate focus group. Fortunately, pressure from Media Matters appears to have caused them to back off. But given how media anchors love to pass along GOP spin as their own words, we still have to fight back hard. The success of Media Matters with MSNBC indicates that we can have an effect if we bother to let these organizations know our displeasure.

For me, first on the list is NPR and Mara Liasson, who were guilty of shoving the Bush flip-flopping lie on Kerry down our throats this morning without any further analysis. Oh, except to mention how "effective" that line has supposedly been. That the thanks for that goes to reporters like her, she does not mention.

September 24, 2004

More on Wilgoren's pseudo-analysis in the New York Times

It turns out that my earlier post about Wilgoren's piece of political pseudo-analysis in today's New York Times was not nearly hard-hitting enough. I failed to mention that the quote from Douglas Brinkley, an author who has written about Kerry's Vietnam service, stretched the bounds of plausibility. Here is what Wilgoren quoted Brinkley as saying:

"Every American now knows that there's something really screwy about George Bush and the National Guard, and they know that John Kerry was not the war hero we thought he was," said Douglas Brinkley, the historian and author of a friendly biography of Mr. Kerry's war years, acknowledging that Mr. Kerry's opponents had succeeded in raising questions about his service.

The part about Kerry immediately struck me as fishy, and no doubt taken out of context. I now wish I had said something about this in my earlier post, as it turns out that Brinkley is indeed quite unhappy with how he was quoted. Enough so that the Kerry campaign issued a press release:

WASHINGTON, Sept. 24 /U.S. Newswire/ -- Author and historian, Douglas Brinkley, issued the following statement to correct a report by the New York Times today:

"A story in the September 24 New York Times leaves the false impression that I think John Kerry was not 'the war hero we thought he was.' Nothing could be further from the truth. He was a great American fighting man in Vietnam and deserved all of his medals. Over the past year I have vigorously defended Kerry's military record and will continue to do so.

"My comment was meant to be about the political consequences of the anti-Kerry Swift boat attacks vs. the anti-Bush National Guard ones. I was speaking about public perceptions not my personal beliefs."

What will the New York Times do now?

Junky analysis from the New York Times

New York Times political writer (I avoid the term "journalist" purposefully) Jodi Wilgoren has an extremely weak, one-sided piece of campaign analysis published today.

In this piece, she manages to re-live for all of us all the supposed "controversy" over the Vietnam-era records of Bush and Kerry. As if the press hadn't focused way too much time on this stuff already. She brings up the Swift Boat Smear Campaign's claims all over again without mentioning any of the abudant evidence that they are completely without merit.

The bigger picture is that these supposed pieces of political "analysis," by major media outlets, are way, way too common. As this one is, they tend to be extremely shallow, with no new reporting, and merely reflect the conventional wisdom of the Washington press corps, which is already dripping all over every other story they write. And which is commonly twisted by spin into some unrecognizable version of reality. This genre should be completely junked.
