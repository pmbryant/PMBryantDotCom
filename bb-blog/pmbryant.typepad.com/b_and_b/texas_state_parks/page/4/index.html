March 23, 2006

Casey on Texas state parks

An excellent summary of the Texas state park system's current troubles was provided yesterday by Rick Casey, former columnist for the San Antonio Express-News and current columnist for the Houston Chronicle. (His move a few years back was a big loss for our local paper.) Some excerpts:

It seemed like a great idea about 15 years ago.

Texas state parks had always been a budgetary stepchild, so the Legislature decided to dedicate some of the money from the tax on sales of sporting goods to fund the parks.

They didn't want to gold-plate the parks, you understand, so the fund would be capped at $32 million. That amount would also include some money for development of city and county parks.

...

But a few years ago, the legislators who couldn't properly fund schools or health insurance for children in low-income families not surprisingly also decided they couldn't properly fund parks.

They cut the $32 million for parks, which had been set way back in 1993 and had not been adjusted for inflation, to about $20 million.

At first the state Department of Parks and Wildlife responded the way organizations typically do when their budgets are pinched.

...

"Last year we ran out of wiggle room," said Parks Director Walt Dabney. "In November we eliminated 73 positions and cut back operations in about 50 parks."

What we have is a parks system that is held together by rubber bands.

The average age of the system's vehicles is 10 years, with an average of about one new vehicle purchase a year in the past four years.

At Choke Canyon there is no potable water because the water system hasn't been repaired. Repairs from Hurricane Rita at the Sabine Pass Battleground State Historical Site and at Martin Dies Jr. State Park near Jasper aren't expected to be completed until summer 2008 at the earliest.

Many facilities are open only Fridays through Sundays, and many have discontinued or curtailed overnight camping.

The B and B team visited Choke Canyon State Park, two hours south of our base, a couple Mondays ago for one of our excellent birding adventures (which I hope to write up something about soon). Fortunately, we had plenty of our own water, as we were surprised to find out there was no water in the park. Now, thanks to Casey, we know the reason why.

I was not surprised to find out that Choke Canyon had eliminated overnight camping, as I had read about that when the cuts hit last fall. But I was surpised and disappointed to discover that we would be kicked out of the campground area near the shoreline of the lake, where we having a very productive and enjoyable birding afternoon, at 4:30 PM—with hours of daylight left.

Choke Canyon is a fine park that deserves better treatment than this. Our state can do better.

(Via By the Bayou.)

March 21, 2006

Texas state parks' troubles finally getting attention?

Is the dire situation of the Texas state park system getting through to the members of the Texas legislature? An article in yesterday's Austin American-Statesman suggests that the answer is 'maybe—but don't count on it.'

Texas state parks are funded primarily by a statewide sales tax on sporting goods, which generates about $105 million a year.

The share for parks, however, has been capped by the Texas Legislature at $32 million, and even that number has been cut during a recent run of tight state budgets. The other $70 million is used for general state spending.

...

Rep. Edmund Kuempel, R-Seguin, former chairman of the House committee responsible for Parks and Wildlife oversight, said there is a chance the department could receive more money next year — but not from the sporting goods tax.

"That I would doubt very seriously," Kuempel said. "Our first step will be to get (funding) back to where we were. Raising the cap is going to be a tough row to hoe.

"We should have been funding (state parks) at the cap level, but as money got tougher, (the Legislature) used the money to balance the budget. There was no anti-Parks and Wildlife sentiment," he said. "We swept funds from a lot of agencies."

If this statement was intended to make parks supporters feel better, I don't think it worked well.

Another Republican representative, Harvey Hildebran of Kerrville, is trying to get the cap on state parks' share of the sporting goods sales tax raised to $85 million. Explaining why he was unable to get that measure passed in the last session, he says:

We had a lot of media and public support for it. Builders and developers were for it. The problem was, we didn't have the support in key positions in committee. The action wasn't as strong as the words.

Builders and developers were for it! It is sadly typical of our GOP legislators (and a few Dems as well) to place such importance on the opinions of real estate developers.

I have to admit that I wasn't fully aware of the crisis in our state parks at the time Hildebran was trying to push his measure through. I was following several issues (out of many potentially disastrous bills) in the state legislature back in the spring of 2005, but this one never hit my radar screen. Next time around will be different, and it will be nice to be on the same side as the 'builders and developers' for once.

For more information, check out Texans for State Parks.

February 03, 2006

Last addition to state park system for a long time?

Thanks to an attentive sister, last Friday I learned that the Dallas Morning-News highlighted the San Antonio area's new wonder of a state park, Government Canyon State Natural Area. And I believe it was at the top of the front page! Should we be expecting a flood of Dallas-ites down here soon?

Here are some highlights from the article:

Its historical but off-putting name sounds like a new prison movie. But Government Canyon, the newest kid on the Texas Parks & Wildlife block, is an 8,622-acre gem, a mother lode of history, geology and scenery that almost went under the bulldozer's blade.

However, thanks to a national scandal and the passion of a small army of Texans, the state natural area in northwest Bexar County escaped development. Twelve years in the making, Government Canyon finally opened its gates in October.

The fact that Government Canyon is one of the state's eight natural areas rather than a park means that conservation, stewardship and education trump recreation. Not that there's a shortage of things to do at the remarkably serene sanctuary only 16 miles from the Alamo City. There are 20 picnic sites, a covered group pavilion with a huge limestone fireplace and barbecue pit and 41 miles of hiking and bike trails.

...

"We tried to keep the development footprint as light as possible," says Ranger Trey Cooksey, the canyon's assistant manager. "People should have places to go that aren't concrete."

...

Given lean state budgets, Government Canyon probably will be the last addition to the Texas parks system for a long time. That makes this tranquil monument to public-private cooperation even more precious.

"All the stars aligned to make Government Canyon," says project manager Jason Corzine. "It's an oasis. Its potential will only be realized when every scrap of land around it is tract houses."

Last addition to the state park system for a long time? Not if Texans for State Parks can help it.

February 01, 2006

Texans for State Parks

The troubled budgetary situation of Texas' state park system that I've written about earlier received some more press last weekend. Both the Dallas Morning-News and the Houston Chronicle, along with at least a few other smaller newspapers, ran an AP article on the issue—an article that also discussed an organization that is attempting to rally the public to support the parks.

For as long as she can remember, Beth McDonald has been a frequent visitor of Texas' state parks. Whether for camping, picnics or afternoon fishing trips, it was easy to load the family into the truck for an outdoor adventure.

"We practically raised our kids at Bastrop State Park," she said.

That's why an ongoing budget battle at the state Capitol, depleted funding and deteriorating conditions at Texas parks have her so upset.

Budget constraints have forced the Texas Parks and Wildlife Department to cut hours and staff at state parks and limit maintenance. The changes have park enthusiasts hopping mad and calling on voters to make boosting funding an election issue.

"There is a firestorm igniting that will be in full force by election time," said McDonald, 79, a retired medical assistant and hospital administrator who now serves as the president of the non-profit Texans for State Parks group.

The group was meeting Friday and Saturday at Bastrop State Park to drum up support for its cause. The meeting should draw hundreds of supporters, McDonald said.

Formed in 1997, Texans for State Parks has about 150 members and another 35 affiliated parks groups around the state.

"We are not going to sit down idly by and watch our once beautiful parks go down the drain," McDonald said.

Texans for State Parks. The organization has been around a few years, but their web site looks brand spanking new. Check them out—and consider helping to stoke the "firestorm" save our parks.

For more, see a mid-January op-ed by none other than Beth McDonald in the Bandera Bulletin.

December 07, 2005

Texas state parks in crisis

In the seven years I have lived in Texas, this state has never been a big spender on public parks. In a state with little in the way of national parks (aside from Big Bend National Park way out west, a full days' drive from the vast majority of Texas' citizens), this shortage of well-funded state parks is particularly acute. The recent, long-delayed, opening of so substantial a new park as Government Canyon State Natural Area, so near to a major metropolital area, was thus very welcome news.

Unfortunately, some unwelcome news is also on the way, as was reported last month:

As the state develops its land the need for green escapes increases. Texas, [Texas Parks and Wildlife commissioner John Parker] said, needs five new parks with 5,000 acres each.

Instead, Parker has watched the state's treasure tarnish with each legislative budget cut.

"The legislature, House and Senate, failed to adequately fund the state park system and told them they were going to cut our budget 5 percent on top of the 15 percent cut the 78th legislature cut it," Parker said.

When state park system's regional directors convened in Austin this past week [early November], Parker was told they were considering drastic cuts to the state park system in order to balance the 2006 budget based on what the legislature had appropriated it. Immediately at stake are the jobs of up to 109 full time state park employees and 100-plus seasonal employees, Parker said. The further reduced budget will also mean closing parks and historical sites a few days a week.

"These sites are located across the state where school children are taken on field trips to see, touch and feel Texas history with their own eyes," Parker said as he drove through Friday morning's early fog en route to Martin Dies Jr. State Park outside Jasper. "The message we are giving with this latest cut that this legislature has forced is 'kids, we as legislators don't think it is important for you to have full advantage of the state historical sites like Caddoan Mounds in Alto or Mission Tejas four miles west of Alto.'"

...

"So now we are at the edge of the cliff because of lack of concern from the state legislature," Parker said. "We'll have to start laying off employees, closing parks, closing historical sites and reducing hours. I just don't think the people of Texas want that to happen."

"Have you seen Arkansas' state parks since they renovated them?" Parker asked on the ride home. This was done by increasing the state sales tax less than a penny, knowing the enhanced green spaces would attract economic development, companies which value places for their employees to decompress. "Their state parks are magnificent!"

Texas' state parks, while too few and too small for our population, are also magnificent, in my limited experience. Just in central Texas, we have, among others, Lost Maples State Natural Area, Garner State Park, Hill Country State Natural Area, Guadalupe River State Park, Enchanted Rock State Natural Area, Pedernales Falls State Park, Palmetto State Park, Choke Canyon State Park. And of course there are the showpieces I've written about before: just outside of San Antonio, Government Canyon State Natural Area, and out in the wilds of west Texas, Big Bend Ranch State Park.

By underfunding our parks, locking them up several days a week and laying off staff, the Texas legislature is ensuring that fewer of the ever-increasing population of this state will be able to enjoy their magnificence. Our quality of life will be diminished. Certainly a great state such as Texas can do better.

Then again, there's a lot of things at which we should be doing better:

AUSTIN - Texas is the only state in the nation that reduced education spending during the 2004-05 school year, according to a report released Monday by the National Education Association.

Per pupil expenditures fell by 1 percent from the previous year, and Texas dropped from 36th to 40th relative to other state's education investments.

So are we a great state or not?

December 01, 2005

The saga of Big Bend Ranch

Seems the Texas businessman who angled to purchase a large section of Big Bend Ranch State Park in west Texas earlier this year, only to be thwarted by a public outcry at the discovery of the secret negotiations and by an ensuing vote of the Texas Parks and Wildlife commissioners, has not given up.

Thanks to the magic of Google News, I have discovered a story that I missed when it was reported by the Houston Chronicle a few weeks ago:

Despite the controversy that erupted over his unsuccessful attempt in August to acquire 46,000 acres from the state park system, [Houston businessman John] Poindexter said last week he may revive his purchase offer after next year's GOP gubernatorial contest, which he claims had a role in dooming the initial deal.

...

The 46,000 acres that the state considered selling him wasn't being sought to expand his resort facilities, as some critics asserted, he added.

"The development potential — as was so frequently cited in the hearing — for this property is as close to zero as anything could reasonably be in the state," he said.

...

Poindexter said he appreciates the public's resistance to selling any parkland.

"An emotion like that is, to me, rational. I wouldn't sell a square foot of Cibolo Creek Ranch," he said.

This Chronicle article is almost entirely about Poindexter and his proclaimed desire to rebuild his reputation and leave a positive legacy. Is it sincere, or part of a greenwash PR-job in advance of his next attempt to purchase the public property?

The Austin American-Statesman followed up with a more complete story on November 2. It goes into more detail about how the potential land deal developed behind-the-scenes:

Contrary to the hurried nature of the final negotiations, Poindexter actually approached the department about buying part of the park more than four years ago. Then-Executive Director Andrew Sansom, citing the fragile and archaeologically significant aspects of the area, rejected the proposal, and the issue died until Poindexter contacted Fitzsimons this year, according to records acquired by the American-Statesman.

Fitzsimons said negotiations with Poindexter regarding a conservation easement he believed would have protected water and Native American sites within the 46,000 acres made the sale worth considering. Negotiations continued almost until the Aug. 25 meeting began. The easement would have placed severe restrictions on Poindexter in terms of water use and required protection of sensitive archaeological sites on the property. The easement also would have allowed Poindexter to develop about 4,600 acres — or 10 percent of the property — along Cienega Creek.

"The (4,600) acres that was being proposed was the worst, from our standpoint," said Lawerence Oaks, executive director of the Texas Historical Commission. "That was the area that was occupied (by native peoples) for 10,000 years."

The potential conservation easement, and what it did not cover, was reported back in August when this news first broke, but it is a useful reminder. How do the exceptions to the easement jive with Poindexter's new claim about the "close to zero" development potential for this land?

If so, why not put conservation easements on the whole thing? 4,600 acres is not a small plot of land—if it were in central Texas, that would be one of our largest parks, all by itself.
