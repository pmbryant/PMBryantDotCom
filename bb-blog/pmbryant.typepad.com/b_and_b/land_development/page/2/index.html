AGUA sues feds to protect Golden-cheeked warbler habitat and the Edwards aquifer

Aquifer Guardians in Urban Areas (AGUA), a San Antonio-based group interested in the protection of the Edwards Aquifer and the land that feeds it, has recently filed a lawsuit charging that the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers and the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service have violated the Endangered Species Act by permitting the construction of an electric transmission line through Golden-cheeked Warbler habitat in northwest Bexar County. Golden-cheeked Warblers are an endangered bird that nests exclusively in central Texas.

From the AGUA website:

In particular, the suit claims that the biological opinion prepared by the Service did not follow federal rules and that the cumulative loss of Golden-cheeked warbler habitat has not been fully and fairly considered. The suit asks that the permit decision be set aside and that a plan be developed and implemented to set aside enough acreage to protect the warbler.

AGUA also reports that "Much of the local habitat has already been eliminated due to suburban developments, especially in the area from Austin to San Antonio." Living in this area, the truth of this is self-evident to me, as I see more and more sections of Hill Country terrain bulldozed and scraped to bedrock every week. It is extremely painful to watch — the slow-motion death of the land we have come to love.

This destruction of land is likely to bring with it the degradation of the principle water source for this entire area, the Edwards Aquifer, as the runoff from this land is what feeds the aquifer. This is the reason that a group like AGUA is concerned with habitat for an endangered bird. AGUA President Enrique Valdivia was quoted in the Express-News last week: "We believe that the warbler is an indicator of the health of the environment and the Edwards Aquifer. Our focus is really to protect the Edwards Aquifer."

December 18, 2006

Update on San Antonio's preservation of land to protect the Edwards Aquifer

Back in 2005, San Antonio voters approved an extension of a 1/8-cent sales tax to be used to purchase land in the recharge zone for the Edwards Aquifer. The goal is to prevent over-development of that land and thus preserve the quality of the water in the underground reservoir that supplies the vast majority of this city's water.

The Houston Chronicle has an update on what is being done with these funds.

It's some of the most picturesque land around this sprawling city: 116 acres of craggy, oak-covered hills near the Government Canyon State Natural Area.

The city bought the acreage in September with nearly $1 million in taxpayer funds — not for parkland but to shield it from development and preserve its role in replenishing the Edwards Aquifer.

The tracts were the first of many being acquired with proceeds from a one-eighth-cent sales tax approved by local voters last year to raise $90 million for water-related land conservation. Most of the acquisitions are likely to be in recharge zone areas of rural Medina and Uvalde counties, where land is cheaper than in heavily urbanized Bexar County.

...

"This is one of the largest, most significant conservation projects in the entire state right now," said Deirdre Hisler, who leads the city advisory board implementing Proposition 1.

...

Rather than buy land, the city prefers to seek easements that impose restrictions on the owners' use of the property but keeps it on the tax rolls.

"We only deal with willing landowners that are willing to be a part of the program," and there is no shortage of interest, Hisler said.

...

Jeff Francell, director of land protection at the Texas Nature Conservancy, said San Antonio's initiative "is going to have a major impact in the future on water quality, on protection of the aquifer and sprawl, to some degree."

By seeking easements in Medina and Uvalde counties, "we're going to be able to protect tens of thousands of acres with the $90 million," Francell said.

"We're talking about land that is in the $1,000-$3,000 an acre range rather than in the $20,000-$50,000 an acre range in Bexar County," Francell said.

The effort should benefit anyone who visits, not just San Antonians, he said.

"A lot of Houston hunts in this area. Just keeping it open for wildlife is an added benefit," he said.

This effort is just one small part of what will be needed to ensure that our quality of life in south and central Texas is preserved in the decades to come.

Off the Kuff also comments on this article, taking issue with the Texas Nature Conservancy's Francell's comment, "The state parks don't have two cents to rub together now. Private buyers are the only hope."

Kuffner writes, "Restoring, or in this case not restoring, those cuts [to state parks budget], is also a choice. Maybe that's a choice you agree with, but it's still a choice. We could choose differently, and that's why Francell's statement about private trusts being the "only hope" is wrong."

I agree that Francell's statement is wrong, but only because he left out local initiatives such as San Antonio's Proposition 1.  At the state government level, I believe Francell is on target, even given the likelihood of increased state parks budgets in the short term (a subject I will be posting about very soon). 

It is inconceivable to me that a state run by Republicans whose ideology and campaign contributors incline them towards underfunding and even selling off state parks is going to embark upon a land acquisition binge any time soon.  And what limited land acquisition by the state we may be fortunate enough to see will likely follow the Government Canyon example of public-private partnerships -- where the Trust for Public Land, and other private organizations, were instrumental in assembling what is now a showpiece of the state parks system.

So, for the foreseeable future, private land trusts and local initiatives such as San Antonio's Proposition 1 appear to be the only substantial hope for preserving undeveloped land.  Of course, I would love to be proved wrong on this.

December 08, 2006

Battle over uncontrolled development in Kendall County

Are some Texas starting to wake up to the necessity to rein in uncontrolled development?  It is looking that way, since a fight is brewing in ultra-conservative Kendall County, in the Hill Country about 30 miles northwest of downtown San Antonio, and the victim of our city's rapidly expanding sea of sprawl.

Real estate developers, accustomed to being able to do as they please in this state, battle even the slightest hint of local control over their behavior.

Aside from my general interest in this subject, I have a personal interest in this area.  When we first moved to the San Antonio area in eight years ago, we rented a small house there, just south of Boerne (pronounced "bernie") for about 18 months, and we still live only a few miles away.  Our current area has recently been overrun by development, and Kendall County, much of which is still open ranchlands outside of the Boerne area , is next on the horizon.

From the San Antonio Express-News yesterday (emphasis added):

Kendall County officials say intense pressure from developers requires more local regulatory power — to help preserve the very lifestyle that draws new residents there.

Two recent lawsuits underscore growing tensions between the county and developers, some of whom contend local leaders are overstepping their authority.

...

Since bills aimed at giving Texas counties greater power have faced perennial defeat in the Legislature, Commissioner Gene Miertschin asked Sen. Jeff Wentworth, R-San Antonio, and Rep.-elect Nathan Macias, R-New Braunfels, to carry a proposal that would apply only to Kendall County.

The bill, which is now being revised, would give county officials power over land use similar to zoning authority, letting them dictate the size, locations, dimensions and uses of buildings in unincorporated areas.

...

Boerne City Councilman Bob Manning said delaying action may prove costly.

"We are on the radar screen of the largest and most sophisticated developers in the nation," he said, quoting consultants hired to update the city's master plan. "We really do need to address the tidal wave of pressure that is coming at us from both east and west on Texas 46."

The county also faces development from the north, via Kerr County, where Greg Blume, builder of Falls Ranch subdivision, wants to tie in to Kendall County roads to reach Interstate 10 in Boerne.

Concerns about road degradation from construction traffic led Kendall commissioners in August to pass regulations that prohibit the use of its roads to access the subdivision.

But the builder ignored the rules, according to a lawsuit the county filed Nov. 16 against Blume, Btex Ranch and Edward Neil Development.

"Once again, a developer is challenging the county's authority to protect the health, safety and welfare of Kendall County's residents," said Allee, the county attorney.

Blume asserts that county leaders are overstepping their authority, and said, "I'm just looking forward to what the judge will say."

The outcome of this brewing battle northwest of here may well decide the fate of this stunning area of the Hill Country.  What will be left of it in 20 years?  In 50 years?

February 20, 2006

San Antonio city council unanimously votes to rein in uncontrolled development

The San Antonio city council is trying to make sure that real estate developers cannot claim exemptions to local laws, aka "vested rights," based on vague documents filed a decade or more ago. Needless to say, real estate developers, who are used to ignoring local laws designed to preserve large trees and protect the quality of water runoff into our aquifers, are not happy about this.

The San Antonio Express-News reported on Friday:

The [unanimous] council vote capped more than a year of hearings by the Vested Rights Task Force and back-and-forth negotiations between developers, environmentalists, the city Planning Commission and city staffers.

The biggest point of contention for developers is a new site plan requirement that must be attached to a project's fair notice form, detailing the project's lot layout, general building footprint with approximate square footage, the amount of impervious cover and how the land will be used.

Developers argued that such a level of detail for single-phase developments usually isn't determined until much later in the life of a project, well after permit applications have been filed.

"Nobody does a site plan at the beginning of the process," said lobbyist Ken Brown, who spoke against the UDC amendments. "What's in place is perfectly fine."

What lobbyist Brown does not mention is that "what's in place" allows developers to claim vested rights by pointing to a virtually blank plat filed decades previously by a different owner (35 years previously in a specific case I posted about last month). "What's in place" allows developers virtually unlimited ability to ignore local laws.

What the city council is now requiring sounds like a reasonable alternative—an alternative that may actually mean that communities have some control over how they grow. The restrictions on developers are hardly unrealistic:

The amendments will allow a developer to make changes to the number of buildings in a project within five years of filing fair notice and maintain vested rights, but only if the change does not increase the impervious cover and it does not increase the total square footage of the original project.

Developers also can make minor adjustments to a building footprint or changes to preserve trees without losing vested rights.

Councilman Art Hall said those provisions represented a middle ground of sorts by allowing some flexibility for developers to alter their plans.

"I think these are amendments both the environmental side and the development side can live with," he said.

Given all the friends that the developers have in the Texas state government, though, I strongly suspect that they will continue to fight for total victory—where communities are powerless to do anything to protect their own citizens from the negative effects of unchecked growth.
